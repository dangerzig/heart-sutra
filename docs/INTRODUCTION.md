# Introduction

## A Multilingual Critical Edition of the Prajñāpāramitāhṛdaya

---

## 1. The Heart Sūtra Problem

The *Prajñāpāramitāhṛdaya*, known in English as the Heart Sūtra, is perhaps the most widely recited Buddhist scripture in East Asia. In fewer than three hundred characters (in its Chinese form), it distills the vast Prajñāpāramitā literature into a concentrated expression of emptiness (*śūnyatā*) doctrine, culminating in the famous mantra *gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā*. Monks and laypeople across China, Korea, Japan, Vietnam, Tibet, and Mongolia have chanted these words daily for over a millennium.

Yet this ubiquitous text presents a fundamental scholarly puzzle: in what language was it composed?

For most of the twentieth century, scholars assumed the Heart Sūtra was composed in Sanskrit and subsequently translated into Chinese by the monk Xuanzang (玄奘, 602–664 CE) during or shortly after his famous pilgrimage to India. Edward Conze's critical editions (1948, 1967, 1975) operated on this assumption, treating Sanskrit manuscripts—primarily from Nepal, dating to the eleventh through nineteenth centuries—as witnesses to an Indian original.

This assumption was challenged decisively by Jan Nattier in her 1992 article "The Heart Sūtra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?" Nattier demonstrated through careful philological analysis that the Heart Sūtra was almost certainly composed in Chinese, with the Sanskrit version representing a later back-translation. Her evidence included:

- **Lexical anomalies**: The Sanskrit text uses *kṣaya* (destruction/exhaustion) where standard Prajñāpāramitā literature consistently uses *nirodha* (cessation). The Chinese character 盡 (*jìn*) can mean either; a translator working from Chinese chose the wrong Sanskrit equivalent.

- **Grammatical artifacts**: Certain Sanskrit constructions appear to be calques from Chinese syntax rather than natural Sanskrit phrasing.

- **Source identification**: The Heart Sūtra's doctrinal core can be traced to specific passages in Kumārajīva's Chinese translation of the Large Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra (T223, 404 CE).

- **Dating evidence**: The earliest securely dated witness is a Chinese inscription on the Fangshan stele (661 CE), while the earliest Sanskrit manuscript (the Hōryū-ji palm-leaf) dates to the seventh or eighth century.

Nattier's thesis, while not universally accepted, has gained substantial scholarly support over the past three decades. Subsequent work by Jayarava Attwood and Huifeng (Shi Huifeng) has refined the argument and identified additional errors in Conze's edition.

---

## 2. The Need for a New Edition

If the Heart Sūtra was composed in Chinese and back-translated into Sanskrit, then existing critical editions—which privilege Sanskrit manuscripts and attempt to reconstruct a Sanskrit "original"—are methodologically flawed. Conze's edition, however influential, presents a text that never existed: a conflation of readings from manuscripts spanning eight centuries, incorporating late Tantric interpolations as if they were original.

This edition takes a different approach. Rather than seeking to reconstruct an archetype, we treat the Heart Sūtra as a **textual complex**—a family of related but distinct texts in Chinese, Sanskrit, and Tibetan, with documented relationships of dependence. Our goals are:

1. **To present T251 as the base text**: Xuanzang's Chinese Heart Sūtra (T251, ca. 649–656 CE) represents the earliest recoverable form of the text.

2. **To document transmission relationships**: Variants are not merely listed but analyzed for direction of dependence (Chinese → Sanskrit, Sanskrit → Tibetan, etc.).

3. **To distinguish recensions**: The short recension (without frame narrative) is original; the long recension (with Buddha entering samādhi, Śāriputra's question, etc.) is a later Sanskrit development.

4. **To identify interpolations**: Elements like the *oṃ* in the mantra, the phrase *na-aprāptiḥ* ("no non-attainment"), and elaborate opening invocations (*maṅgala*) appear only in later witnesses and are marked accordingly.

5. **To provide multilingual access**: Chinese, Sanskrit (in both Devanagari and IAST), and Tibetan texts are presented in parallel alignment.

## 3. Scope and Limitations

This edition focuses on the primary witnesses to the Heart Sūtra tradition:

**Chinese**: The Taishō Canon texts (T250, T251, T256, T257), the Fangshan stele inscription (661 CE), and representative Dunhuang manuscripts.

**Sanskrit**: The Hōryū-ji palm-leaf manuscript, the Nepalese manuscript tradition, and the GRETIL digital edition (which follows Conze with corrections).

**Tibetan**: The Degé Kangyur (Toh 21) and variant readings from other Kangyur editions.

We acknowledge significant limitations:

- We have worked primarily from published editions and digital texts rather than conducting fresh manuscript collation. A full critical edition would require examination of physical manuscripts and rubbings.

- The Dunhuang manuscripts are cataloged but not collated character-by-character.

- Korean, Japanese (beyond Hōryū-ji), Xixia, Khotanese, and Uyghur traditions are not included.

- The question of T250's date and attribution remains unresolved.

We therefore describe this work as a **preliminary critical apparatus** rather than a definitive critical edition. It is intended as a foundation for further research rather than a final word.

## 4. Original Contributions

While building on the work of Nattier, Attwood, Huifeng, and others, this edition offers several contributions:

### 4.1 The Parallel Composition Hypothesis

We propose that the two Chinese short-recension Heart Sūtras—T250 (大明咒經, attributed to Kumārajīva) and T251 (心經, attributed to Xuanzang)—are not stages in a single text's revision history but **parallel compositions** from common source material.

Evidence includes:

- T250 contains passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation) absent from T251. Revision typically adds material; deletion of doctrinal content is unusual.

- T250's three-epithet mantra formula exactly matches the Large Sūtra (T223); T251 innovates with four epithets. This suggests independent extraction rather than revision.

- The texts use internally consistent but mutually incompatible terminology (pre-Xuanzang vs. Xuanzang-era translation conventions).

We present this as a hypothesis requiring further investigation, not an established conclusion.

### 4.2 Direction-of-Dependence Apparatus

The critical apparatus explicitly encodes transmission direction:

- **C→S**: Chinese to Sanskrit (back-translation)
- **S→T**: Sanskrit to Tibetan
- **S→C**: Sanskrit to Chinese (T257's reverse transmission)
- **⊥**: Independent development

This allows readers to understand not just what variants exist but how they relate historically.

### 4.3 Digital Infrastructure

The edition is built on structured JSON data models that enable:

- Programmatic querying of witnesses and variants
- Alignment across traditions at the segment level
- Future extension to additional witnesses
- Export to multiple formats (LaTeX, potentially TEI-XML)

## 5. A Note on Scholarly Debate

We adopt the Chinese compositional priority thesis as our working framework, but we acknowledge that this position is not universally accepted. Scholars including Fukui Fumimasa and Harada Waso have defended aspects of the Sanskrit-origin position. The philological evidence, in our judgment, strongly favors Chinese priority, but readers should be aware that the question remains open in some scholarly circles.

Similarly, our parallel composition hypothesis for T250/T251 is tentative. Alternative interpretations exist: T250 could be a later archaizing abridgment, or T251 could be a revision that deliberately dropped material. We have not definitively ruled out these possibilities.

Scholarly humility requires acknowledging what we do not know:

- Who created the Sanskrit back-translation, and where?
- What is T250's actual date of composition?
- Was there a proto-Heart Sūtra underlying both T250 and T251?
- When and why were the long-recension frame narratives added?

We hope this edition provides resources for addressing these questions rather than claiming to have answered them.

## 6. How to Use This Edition

### For General Readers

The parallel editions in `output/latex/` provide readable critical texts:

- `chinese_critical.pdf`: T251 with apparatus and T250 comparisons
- `sanskrit_critical.pdf`: Corrected Sanskrit text with back-translation analysis
- `tibetan_critical.pdf`: Degé Kangyur text with Wylie transliteration
- `parallel_complete.pdf`: All three traditions in synoptic presentation

The English translation in `output/translation/` provides an annotated rendering with philological notes.

### For Scholars

The `data/` directory contains structured JSON files for each witness, enabling programmatic analysis. The `variant_table.json` documents significant variants with direction-of-dependence analysis.

The methodology documentation in `docs/METHODOLOGY_FULL.md` explains our editorial principles in detail.

### For Digital Humanities

The Python library in `src/hrdaya/` provides data models and utilities for working with the edition programmatically. The witness catalog (`witnesses.py`) documents over forty witnesses with full metadata.
