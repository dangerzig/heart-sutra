# Research Findings

## Prajñāpāramitāhṛdaya Critical Edition Project

---

## Executive Summary

This document presents the findings of systematic textual analysis conducted for the Heart Sūtra critical edition. We distinguish between:

1. **Original findings** — New contributions to scholarship
2. **Confirmatory findings** — Evidence supporting existing scholarship
3. **Corrections** — Errors in received editions now documented

---

## Part I: Original Findings

### Finding 1: T250 and T251 Are Parallel Compositions

**Claim:** The two Chinese short-recension Heart Sūtras (T250 and T251) are not stages in a single text's revision history but independent compositions from common source material.

**Evidence:**

| Observation | Implication |
|-------------|-------------|
| T250 has skandha-characteristic passage absent from T251 | Revision would not delete doctrinal elaboration |
| T250 has temporal negation absent from T251 | Same — deletion of doctrine unlikely |
| T250 has 3 epithets; T251 has 4 | Different compositional choice, not update |
| T250 terminology internally consistent (pre-Xuanzang) | Composed as a unit |
| T251 terminology internally consistent (Xuanzang-era) | Composed as a unit |
| Different title framing (明呪經 vs 心經) | Different generic conception |

**T250 Unique Passages:**

```
色空故無惱壞相，受空故無受相，想空故無知相，
行空故無作相，識空故無覺相。
("Because form is empty there is no characteristic of affliction...")
```

```
是空法，非過去，非未來，非現在。
("This emptiness-dharma is not past, not future, not present.")
```

These passages represent substantive doctrinal content. A reviser creating T251 from T250 would have no reason to delete them. Their absence from T251 suggests T251 was composed independently, not revised from T250.

**Revised Stemma:**

```
            α (Large Sūtra T223)
           ╱                    ╲
          ↓                      ↓
    β (proto-text?)              Ω (Xuanzang composition)
          ↓                      ↓
        T250                   T251
      (parallel)             (parallel)
```

**Significance:** If correct, this changes our understanding of Heart Sūtra origins. Rather than a single Chinese text revised over time, we may have evidence for two distinct Chinese Heart Sūtra compositions, possibly from different Buddhist circles.

**Alternative Interpretations:**

We acknowledge that this hypothesis has not been definitively established. Alternative interpretations include:

1. **T250 as later abridgment:** T250 could be a later text that archaizes terminology to appear older. The three-epithet pattern could be deliberate simplification, not preservation.

2. **T251 as revision with deletion:** Revisers do sometimes abridge. The unique T250 passages could have been deliberately omitted in T251 for concision.

3. **Common proto-text:** Both T250 and T251 could derive from a common ancestor (β) that we cannot reconstruct.

4. **Different recension traditions:** The differences could reflect regional variations rather than independent composition.

Further research is needed, particularly systematic comparison with other Buddhist excerpt/digest texts (抄經) to establish what revisers typically did and did not do.

**Status:** This hypothesis requires further development. Nattier (1992) discusses T250 primarily for dating evidence. We have not conducted exhaustive searches of Japanese and Chinese scholarship to confirm whether this specific analysis has been previously proposed.

### Finding 2: T250's Epithet Formula Exactly Matches the Large Sūtra

**Claim:** T250's three-epithet formula preserves the original Large Sūtra pattern, while T251's four-epithet formula is an innovation.

**Evidence:**

| Source | Epithet Formula |
|--------|-----------------|
| **T223 (Large Sūtra)** | 是大明呪、無上明呪、無等等明呪 |
| **T250** | 是大明呪、無上明呪、無等等明呪 |
| **T251** | 是大神咒、是大明咒、是無上咒、是無等等咒 |
| **Sanskrit** | mahāmantro mahāvidyāmantro 'nuttaramantro 'samasama-mantraḥ |

Observations:
1. T250 exactly matches T223 (same characters, same structure)
2. T251 adds a fourth epithet (大神咒)
3. T251 drops 明 from two epithets (無上咒, 無等等咒)
4. Sanskrit has four epithets, matching T251 not T250

**Interpretation:**
- T250 directly excerpts from T223 without modification
- T251 innovates by adding 神咒 and restructuring
- The Sanskrit four-epithet formula was back-translated from T251, not from T250

**Significance:** T250 is closer to the source material in this respect. The commonly used T251 represents a revision of the epithet tradition, not the original form.

**Status:** Attwood (2017b) discusses the epithets extensively. Our contribution is the systematic tabular comparison showing exact T250-T223 correspondence. Whether this specific observation has been made elsewhere in Japanese or Chinese scholarship requires further investigation.

### Finding 3: "Form is Emptiness" is Creative Synthesis, Not Excerpt

**Claim:** The Heart Sūtra's famous formula "form is emptiness, emptiness is form" is not excerpted from the Large Sūtra but creatively synthesized from a different formula.

**Evidence:**

| Text | Formula |
|------|---------|
| **Aṣṭasāhasrikā** | rūpam eva **māyā** māyaiva rūpam |
| | ("form is only illusion, illusion is only form") |
| **Heart Sūtra** | rūpaṃ **śūnyatā** śūnyataiva rūpam |
| | ("form is emptiness, emptiness is form") |

The Large Prajñāpāramitā sūtras use **māyā** (illusion) in this formula, not **śūnyatā** (emptiness). The Heart Sūtra composer transformed the māyā-formula into a śūnyatā-formula.

**Interpretation:** The Heart Sūtra is not a mere "digest" (抄經) that excerpts passages unchanged. It is a creative recomposition that synthesizes Prajñāpāramitā themes into new formulations.

**Significance:** This affects how we understand the Heart Sūtra's composition. The author was not merely copying but actively composing, transforming source material to create new doctrinal expressions.

**Status:** Nattier (1992) discusses excerption methodology. The māyā→śūnyatā observation may be implicit in existing scholarship; we highlight it explicitly here but do not claim it as entirely original.

## Part II: Confirmatory Findings

### Confirmation 1: kṣaya/nirodha Confirms Back-Translation

**Finding:** Systematic comparison confirms the kṣaya/nirodha discrepancy across all witnesses.

| Witness Type | Term Used | Expected Term |
|--------------|-----------|---------------|
| Large Sūtra (Sanskrit) | nirodha | — |
| Large Sūtra (T223 Chinese) | 盡 (jìn) | — |
| Heart Sūtra (Chinese) | 盡 (jìn) | — |
| Heart Sūtra (Sanskrit) | **kṣaya** | nirodha |
| Heart Sūtra (Tibetan) | **zad pa** | 'gog pa |

The Tibetan zad pa means "exhaustion" (= kṣaya), not "cessation" (= nirodha). This confirms:
1. The Tibetan was translated from Sanskrit (preserving kṣaya error)
2. The Sanskrit was back-translated from Chinese (mistranslating 盡)
3. The chain is: Chinese → Sanskrit → Tibetan

**Status:** Confirms Nattier (1992).

---

### Confirmation 2: Dunhuang Evidence Supports T251 Dominance

**Finding:** Analysis of Dunhuang manuscript catalog shows:

| Manuscript | Type | Date | Notes |
|------------|------|------|-------|
| S.2464 | T251-type | 7th-8th c. | Matches T251 |
| P.2168 | T251-type | 8th-9th c. | Matches T251 |
| P.2323 | T251-type | **851 CE** | Dated colophon |
| S.700 | **T250-type** | 7th-8th c. | Pre-Xuanzang terminology |
| P.3917 | Bilingual | 8th-9th c. | Sanskrit transliteration |

**Interpretation:**
- T251-type texts dominate the Dunhuang collection
- P.2323's dated colophon (851 CE) provides secure terminus ante quem
- T250-type texts survived (S.700) but were minority
- No T257-type (long recension) found, suggesting it wasn't known at Dunhuang before sealing (ca. 1000 CE)

**Status:** Manuscript catalogs are known; this systematic classification for Heart Sūtra purposes is useful consolidation.

### Confirmation 3: Late Interpolations Identified

**Finding:** Comparison of early vs. late witnesses confirms interpolation of:

#### a) oṃ in mantra

| Witness | Date | oṃ present? |
|---------|------|-------------|
| T251 | 649-656 | No |
| Fangshan | 661 | No |
| Hōryūji | 7th-8th c. | No |
| T256 | 8th-9th c. | No |
| Na | 11th c. | No |
| Nb | 17th-18th c. | **Yes** |
| Ne | 15th-17th c. | **Yes** |
| T257 | 1005 | **Yes** |

**Conclusion:** oṃ is a Tantric addition (9th-10th c.), absent from all early witnesses.

#### b) na-aprāptiḥ ("no non-attainment")

| Witness | na-aprāptiḥ present? |
|---------|---------------------|
| T251 | No (無智亦無得) |
| Fangshan | No |
| Hōryūji | No |
| T256 | Yes |
| T257 | Yes (亦無無得) |
| Late Nepalese | Yes |
| Conze edition | Yes |

**Conclusion:** na-aprāptiḥ is interpolation, absent from earliest Chinese and Sanskrit witnesses.

#### c) Elaborate maṅgala

| Witness | Maṅgala form |
|---------|--------------|
| T251 | None |
| Hōryūji | namas sarvajñāya (simple) |
| Late Nepalese | Oṃ namo Bhagavatyai Ārya-Prajñāpāramitāyai! (elaborate) |

**Conclusion:** Elaborate Tantric maṅgala is late; simple form is earlier.

**Status:** Confirms Attwood (2021), Huifeng (2014).

## Part III: Tibetan Witness Analysis

### Finding: All Tibetan Editions Share Sanskrit-Derived Features

| Feature | Present in Tibetan? | Implication |
|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| zad pa (= kṣaya) | Yes, all editions | Translated from Sanskrit |
| oṃ in mantra | Yes, all editions | Long recension Sanskrit source |
| ma thob pa (= na-aprāptiḥ) | Yes, all editions | Interpolation in Sanskrit source |
| Frame narrative | Yes, all editions | Long recension |

**Interpretation:** Tibetan was translated from Sanskrit long recension that already contained Tantric elements (oṃ) and interpolations (na-aprāptiḥ). Tibetan is not an independent witness to the Chinese text but a secondary witness to the Sanskrit tradition.

**Variant patterns across editions:**
- Degé, Peking, Narthang, Lhasa show minor orthographic variants
- No substantive doctrinal differences found
- All preserve same translation (Vimalamitra/Rinchen Dé)

**Status:** Confirms Silk (1994); provides systematic comparison.

## Part IV: Large Sūtra Parallel Analysis

### Source Passage Identification

The Heart Sūtra's doctrinal core corresponds to Pañcaviṃśatisāhasrikā, Chapter 3 (Gilgit MS folios 20v-22v), translated by Kumārajīva as T223.

| Heart Sūtra Section | Large Sūtra Parallel | Transformation |
|---------------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Skandha negation | rūpaṃ śūnyam... | "X is empty" → "no X" |
| Āyatana negation | cakṣuḥ śūnyam... | Same pattern |
| Dhātu negation | cakṣur-dhātuḥ śūnyaḥ... | Same pattern |
| Pratītyasamutpāda | avidyā śūnyā... avidyā-**nirodhaḥ** śūnyaḥ | nirodha → kṣaya error |
| Four Truths | duḥkhaṃ śūnyam... | Same pattern |
| Wisdom/attainment | na jñānaṃ na prāptir **nābhisamayaḥ** | nābhisamayaḥ omitted |

### Key Finding: Consistent Transformation Method

The Heart Sūtra composer used a consistent method:
- Large Sūtra: "X is empty" (X śūnyam)
- Heart Sūtra: "no X" (na X / 無X)

This is not random excerption but systematic transformation.

### Key Finding: T223 Transmission Error Preserved

The Heart Sūtra preserves Kumārajīva's T223 reading 無智亦無得 (no wisdom, no attainment), which omits nābhisamayaḥ (no realization) present in Sanskrit Large Sūtra. This confirms:
1. Heart Sūtra composer used T223 specifically
2. Not working from Sanskrit directly
3. T223's omission was inherited

**Status:** Confirms Huifeng (2014); provides systematic mapping.

## Part VI: Outstanding Questions

### Questions Requiring Further Research

1. **What is T250's actual date?**
   - Attribution to Kumārajīva (402-412) is disputed
   - Terminology suggests pre-Xuanzang
   - But could be contemporaneous parallel composition

2. **Is there a common ancestor (β) for T250 and T251?**
   - Both use Large Sūtra material
   - Could there be an earlier proto-Heart Sūtra?
   - No direct evidence yet

3. **When exactly was the Sanskrit back-translation made?**
   - Must be before Hōryūji MS (7th-8th c.)
   - Probably mid-7th century
   - Who made it? In China or Central Asia?

4. **What do the unexamined Dunhuang manuscripts contain?**
   - Full collation of S.700 would clarify T250 tradition
   - Other fragments may exist
   - Central Asian manuscripts not yet cataloged

5. **Are there Sanskrit witnesses preserving T250-type readings?**
   - All known Sanskrit matches T251
   - If T250 was also back-translated, evidence is lost

---

## Conclusion

This critical edition proposes three areas for further investigation:

1. **T250/T251 as parallel compositions** — a hypothesis challenging the revision model, requiring further research into Buddhist excerpt/digest practices
2. **T250 epithet priority** — systematic documentation of T250-T223 correspondence
3. **māyā→śūnyatā transformation** — explicit highlighting of the Heart Sūtra's creative synthesis

It confirms and consolidates:
- Chinese compositional priority (Nattier), while acknowledging ongoing scholarly debate
- Corrections to Conze's edition (Attwood, Huifeng)
- Late interpolation of oṃ, na-aprāptiḥ, elaborate maṅgala
- Sanskrit kṣaya/nirodha error as back-translation evidence

On the balance of evidence, the Heart Sūtra appears to be a Chinese Buddhist composition that was back-translated to Sanskrit, elaborated with Tantric elements, and transmitted across Asia in multiple recensions. This interpretation, while well-supported, remains subject to scholarly debate.
