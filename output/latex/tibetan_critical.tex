\section*{Transmission History}

The Tibetan Heart Sūtra exists in two recensions. The Kangyur text (Toh 21) was translated from Sanskrit during the early translation period (8th--9th century) by Vimalamitra and Rinchen Dé (rin chen sde). It represents the \textbf{long recension} with frame narrative, translated from a Sanskrit text that already contained:

\begin{itemize}
\item The frame narrative (nidāna and epilogue)
\item The oṃ in the mantra
\item The na-aprāptiḥ interpolation (\tib{མ་ཐོབ་པ་ཡང་མེད})
\item The zad pa (= kṣaya) error inherited from Sanskrit
\end{itemize}

A \textbf{short recension} is attested by IOL Tib J 751, a Dunhuang manuscript dated c.\ 823 CE --- the earliest securely datable Tibetan Heart Sūtra witness. This short text lacks the frame narrative and corresponds to Chinese T251 and the Hōryūji Sanskrit manuscript. Its imperial-period orthography may preserve older translation conventions. The relationship between the Dunhuang short recension and the Kangyur long recension is not that of draft and revision, but likely reflects translation from different Sanskrit source texts (short vs.\ long).

The Tibetan is a \textbf{tertiary witness}: Chinese $\to$ Sanskrit $\to$ Tibetan. It preserves features of the Sanskrit back-translation, including errors that trace back to Chinese.

\section*{Relationship to Prior Editions}

Critical study of the Tibetan Heart Sūtra has largely been confined to Conze's use of Kangyur readings (1967), the ACIP/BUDA digital corpus, and individual manuscript studies. Attwood's research programme (2015--2024) focuses primarily on the Chinese and Sanskrit texts but has identified key features inherited by the Tibetan:

\begin{itemize}
\item The \textit{na-apr\=apti\d{h}} interpolation, present in Sanskrit and carried into the Tibetan (\tib{མ་ཐོབ་པ་ཡང་མེད})
\item The \textit{k\d{s}aya}/\textit{zad pa} reading, shared with late Sanskrit manuscripts
\item The Tantric additions (o\d{m}, frame narrative) indicating translation from a long-recension Sanskrit source
\end{itemize}

Attwood does not publish a Tibetan edition; his focus is on repairing the Chinese and Sanskrit texts. No prior critical edition collates the Kangyur witnesses against each other or includes the Dunhuang short recension.

This edition makes two contributions: (1) collation of six Kangyur witnesses (Degé, Peking, Narthang, Lhasa, Lithang, Stog Palace) to establish the Tibetan textual tradition, and (2) inclusion of IOL Tib J 751 (c.\ 823 CE), the Dunhuang short recension, as a witness --- the earliest securely datable Tibetan Heart Sūtra text. As with the companion Chinese and Sanskrit editions, this is a \emph{critical} edition presenting the received text with variants in the apparatus, not a revised edition proposing emendations.

\end{description}

\subsection*{Nidāna (Frame Narrative Opening)}

\tib{རྒྱ་གར་སྐད་དུ། བྷ་ག་ཝ་ཏཱི་པྲཛྙཱ་པཱ་ར་མི་ཏཱ་ཧྲྀ་ད་ཡ།}\\
\wylie{rgya gar skad du | bha ga wa tI praj+nyA pA ra mi tA h+ri da ya |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\tib{བོད་སྐད་དུ། བཅོམ་ལྡན་འདས་མ་ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པའི་སྙིང་པོ།}\\
\wylie{bod skad du | bcom ldan 'das ma shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i snying po |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\tib{འདི་སྐད་བདག་གིས་ཐོས་པ་དུས་གཅིག་ན།}\\
\wylie{'di skad bdag gis thos pa dus gcig na |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\tib{བཅོམ་ལྡན་འདས་རྒྱལ་པོའི་ཁབ་བྱ་རྒོད་ཕུང་པོའི་རི་ལ།}\\
\wylie{bcom ldan 'das rgyal po'i khab bya rgod phung po'i ri la |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\tib{དགེ་སློང་གི་དགེ་འདུན་ཆེན་པོ་དང་།}\\
\wylie{dge slong gi dge 'dun chen po dang |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\tib{བྱང་ཆུབ་སེམས་དཔའི་དགེ་འདུན་ཆེན་པོ་དང་ཐབས་གཅིག་ཏུ་བཞུགས་ཏེ།}\\
\wylie{byang chub sems dpa'i dge 'dun chen po dang thabs gcig tu bzhugs te |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{rgya gar skad du (in the language of India): [Sanskrit title]. bod skad du (in Tibetan): bcom ldan 'das ma (Bhagavatī) shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i (of the perfection of wisdom) snying po (heart). 'di skad (thus) bdag gis (by me) thos pa (heard) dus gcig na (at one time): bcom ldan 'das (the Bhagavān) rgyal po'i khab (in Rājagṛha) bya rgod phung po'i ri la (on Vulture Peak Mountain), dge slong gi dge 'dun chen po (great assembly of monks) dang (and) byang chub sems dpa'i dge 'dun chen po (great assembly of bodhisattvas) dang thabs gcig tu (together with) bzhugs te (was dwelling).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{In the language of India: Bhagavatī Prajñāpāramitā Hṛdaya. In Tibetan: Heart of the Bhagavatī Perfection of Wisdom. Thus have I heard at one time: the Bhagavān was dwelling on Vulture Peak Mountain in Rājagṛha, together with a great assembly of monks and a great assembly of bodhisattvas.}

\subsection*{Core Text}

\linenum{1} \tib{འཕགས་པ་སྤྱན་རས་གཟིགས་དབང་ཕྱུག་བྱང་ཆུབ་སེམས་དཔའ་སེམས་དཔའ་ཆེན་པོ།}\\
\wylie{'phags pa spyan ras gzigs dbang phyug byang chub sems dpa' sems dpa' chen po |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{2} \tib{ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པ་ཟབ་མོའི་སྤྱོད་པ་སྤྱད་པ།}\\
\wylie{shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa zab mo'i spyod pa spyad pa |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{3} \tib{ཕུང་པོ་ལྔ་པོ་དེ་དག་ལ་ཡང་རང་བཞིན་གྱིས་སྟོང་པར་རྣམ་པར་བལྟའོ།}\\
\wylie{phung po lnga po de dag la yang rang bzhin gyis stong par rnam par blta'o |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{'phags pa (noble) spyan ras gzigs dbang phyug (Avalokiteśvara) byang chub sems dpa' sems dpa' chen po (bodhisattva mahāsattva), shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa (prajñāpāramitā) zab mo'i (profound) spyod pa (practice) spyad pa (was practicing): phung po lnga po (five aggregates) de dag (those) la yang (also upon) rang bzhin gyis (by intrinsic nature) stong par (as empty) rnam par blta'o (looked).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{The noble Avalokiteśvara, bodhisattva mahāsattva, was practicing the profound prajñāpāramitā, and he looked upon the five aggregates and saw them as empty of intrinsic nature.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{4} \tib{ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ། གཟུགས་སྟོང་པའོ། སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་གཟུགས་སོ།}\\
\wylie{shA ri'i bu | gzugs stong pa'o | stong pa nyid gzugs so |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{5} \tib{གཟུགས་ལས་སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་གཞན་མ་ཡིན། སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་ལས་ཀྱང་གཟུགས་གཞན་མ་ཡིན་ནོ།}\\
\wylie{gzugs las stong pa nyid gzhan ma yin | stong pa nyid las kyang gzugs gzhan ma yin no |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{6} \tib{དེ་བཞིན་དུ་ཚོར་བ་དང་འདུ་ཤེས་དང་འདུ་བྱེད་དང་རྣམ་པར་ཤེས་པ་རྣམས་སྟོང་པའོ།}\\
\wylie{de bzhin du tshor ba dang 'du shes dang 'du byed dang rnam par shes pa rnams stong pa'o |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{shA ri'i bu (Śāriputra): gzugs (form) stong pa'o (is empty); stong pa nyid (emptiness) gzugs so (is form). gzugs las (from form) stong pa nyid (emptiness) gzhan ma yin (is not other); stong pa nyid las kyang (from emptiness also) gzugs (form) gzhan ma yin no (is not other). de bzhin du (likewise) tshor ba (feeling) dang (and) 'du shes (perception) dang (and) 'du byed (formation) dang (and) rnam par shes pa rnams (consciousnesses) stong pa'o (are empty).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{Śāriputra, form is empty; emptiness is form. Emptiness is not other than form; form is not other than emptiness. Likewise, feeling, perception, formation, and consciousness are empty.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{7} \tib{ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ། དེ་ལྟར་ཆོས་ཐམས་ཅད་སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་དེ། མཚན་ཉིད་མེད་པ།}\\
\wylie{shA ri'i bu | de ltar chos thams cad stong pa nyid de | mtshan nyid med pa |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{8} \tib{མ་སྐྱེས་པ། མ་འགགས་པ། དྲི་མ་མེད་པ། དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ། བྲི་བ་མེད་པ། གང་བ་མེད་པའོ།}\\
\wylie{ma skyes pa | ma 'gags pa | dri ma med pa | dri ma dang bral ba med pa | bri ba med pa | gang ba med pa'o |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{shA ri'i bu (Śāriputra): de ltar (thus) chos thams cad (all dharmas) stong pa nyid de (are emptiness), mtshan nyid med pa (without characteristics); ma skyes pa (not arisen), ma 'gags pa (not ceased); dri ma med pa (not defiled), dri ma dang bral ba med pa (not free from defilement); bri ba med pa (not decreasing), gang ba med pa'o (not increasing).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{Śāriputra, thus all dharmas are emptiness: without characteristics, not arisen, not ceased, not defiled, not free from defilement, not decreasing, not increasing.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{9} \tib{ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ། དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་ལ་གཟུགས་མེད། ཚོར་བ་མེད། འདུ་ཤེས་མེད། འདུ་བྱེད་རྣམས་མེད། རྣམ་པར་ཤེས་པ་མེད།}\\
\wylie{shA ri'i bu | de lta bas na stong pa nyid la gzugs med | tshor ba med | 'du shes med | 'du byed rnams med | rnam par shes pa med |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{10} \tib{མིག་མེད། རྣ་བ་མེད། སྣ་མེད། ལྕེ་མེད། ལུས་མེད། ཡིད་མེད།}\\
\wylie{mig med | rna ba med | sna med | lce med | lus med | yid med |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{11} \tib{གཟུགས་མེད། སྒྲ་མེད། དྲི་མེད། རོ་མེད། རེག་བྱ་མེད། ཆོས་མེད་དོ།}\\
\wylie{gzugs med | sgra med | dri med | ro med | reg bya med | chos med do |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{12} \tib{མིག་གི་ཁམས་མེད་པ་ནས་ཡིད་ཀྱི་རྣམ་པར་ཤེས་པའི་ཁམས་ཀྱི་བར་དུ་ཡང་མེད་དོ།}\\
\wylie{mig gi khams med pa nas yid kyi rnam par shes pa'i khams kyi bar du yang med do |}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{13} \tib{མ་རིག་པ་མེད། མ་རིག་པ་ཟད་པ་མེད་པ་ནས།}\footnote{\lemma{\tib{ཟད་པ}} \textit{zad pa} ``exhaustion''] This translates Sanskrit \textit{kṣaya}, \emph{not} the correct \textit{nirodha} (which would be \tib{འགོག་པ} \textit{'gog pa}). The Tibetan preserves the Sanskrit back-translation error, confirming the transmission chain: Chinese 盡 → Sanskrit \textit{kṣaya} → Tibetan \textit{zad pa}.}\\
\wylie{ma rig pa med | ma rig pa zad pa med pa nas |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{14} \tib{རྒ་ཤི་མེད། རྒ་ཤི་ཟད་པའི་བར་དུ་ཡང་མེད་དོ།}\\
\wylie{rga shi med | rga shi zad pa'i bar du yang med do |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{15} \tib{སྡུག་བསྔལ་བ་དང་། ཀུན་འབྱུང་བ་དང་། འགོག་པ་དང་། ལམ་མེད།}\\
\wylie{sdug bsngal ba dang | kun 'byung ba dang | 'gog pa dang | lam med |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{16} \tib{ཡེ་ཤེས་མེད། ཐོབ་པ་མེད། མ་ཐོབ་པ་ཡང་མེད་དོ།}\footnote{\lemma{\tib{མ་ཐོབ་པ་ཡང་མེད}} \textit{ma thob pa yang med}] ``No non-attainment.'' This translates Sanskrit \textit{na-aprāptiḥ}, which is absent from the earliest Chinese (T251, Fangshan) and Sanskrit (Hōryūji) witnesses. It is a late interpolation that entered the Tibetan through its Sanskrit source.}\\
\wylie{ye shes med | thob pa med | ma thob pa yang med do |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{shA ri'i bu (Śāriputra): de lta bas na (therefore) stong pa nyid la (in emptiness) gzugs med (no form), tshor ba med (no feeling), 'du shes med (no perception), 'du byed rnams med (no formations), rnam par shes pa med (no consciousness); mig med (no eye), rna ba med (no ear), sna med (no nose), lce med (no tongue), lus med (no body), yid med (no mind); gzugs med (no form), sgra med (no sound), dri med (no smell), ro med (no taste), reg bya med (no tangible), chos med do (no mental object). mig gi khams med pa (no eye element) nas (from) yid kyi rnam par shes pa'i khams kyi bar du yang med do (up to no mind-consciousness element also not). ma rig pa med (no ignorance), ma rig pa zad pa med pa (no exhaustion of ignorance) nas (up to) rga shi med (no old age and death), rga shi zad pa'i bar du yang med do (no exhaustion of old age and death). sdug bsngal ba (suffering) dang (and) kun 'byung ba (origination) dang (and) 'gog pa (cessation) dang (and) lam med (no path). ye shes med (no wisdom), thob pa med (no attainment), ma thob pa yang med do (no non-attainment also).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{Śāriputra, therefore in emptiness there is no form, no feeling, no perception, no formations, no consciousness; no eye, no ear, no nose, no tongue, no body, no mind; no form, no sound, no smell, no taste, no tangible, no mental object. No eye-element up to no mind-consciousness element. No ignorance, no exhaustion of ignorance, up to no old age and death, no exhaustion of old age and death. No suffering, no origination, no cessation, no path; no wisdom, no attainment, and no non-attainment.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{17} \tib{ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ། དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་བྱང་ཆུབ་སེམས་དཔའ་རྣམས་ཐོབ་པ་མེད་པའི་ཕྱིར།}\\
\wylie{shA ri'i bu | de lta bas na byang chub sems dpa' rnams thob pa med pa'i phyir |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{18} \tib{ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པ་ལ་བརྟེན་ཅིང་གནས་ཏེ།}\\
\wylie{shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa la brten cing gnas te |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{19} \tib{སེམས་ལ་སྒྲིབ་པ་མེད་པས་འཇིགས་པ་མེད་དེ།}\\
\wylie{sems la sgrib pa med pas 'jigs pa med de |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{20} \tib{ཕྱིན་ཅི་ལོག་ལས་ཤིན་ཏུ་འདས་ནས་མྱ་ངན་ལས་འདས་པའི་མཐར་ཕྱིན་ཏོ།}\\
\wylie{phyin ci log las shin tu 'das nas mya ngan las 'das pa'i mthar phyin to |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{shA ri'i bu (Śāriputra): de lta bas na (therefore) byang chub sems dpa' rnams (bodhisattvas) thob pa med pa'i phyir (because there is no attainment), shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa la (on prajñāpāramitā) brten cing (relying) gnas te (abiding). sems la (in the mind) sgrib pa med pas (because there is no obscuration) 'jigs pa med de (there is no fear); phyin ci log las (from error) shin tu 'das nas (having completely transcended) mya ngan las 'das pa'i (of nirvāṇa) mthar phyin to (they arrive at the end).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{Śāriputra, therefore, because there is no attainment, bodhisattvas rely on and abide in prajñāpāramitā. Because their minds have no obscurations, they have no fear. Having completely transcended error, they attain the final nirvāṇa.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{21} \tib{དུས་གསུམ་དུ་རྣམ་པར་བཞུགས་པའི་སངས་རྒྱས་ཐམས་ཅད་ཀྱང་ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པ་ལ་བརྟེན་ནས།}\\
\wylie{dus gsum du rnam par bzhugs pa'i sangs rgyas thams cad kyang shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa la brten nas |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{22} \tib{བླ་ན་མེད་པ་ཡང་དག་པར་རྫོགས་པའི་བྱང་ཆུབ་ཏུ་མངོན་པར་རྫོགས་པར་སངས་རྒྱས་སོ།}\\
\wylie{bla na med pa yang dag par rdzogs pa'i byang chub tu mngon par rdzogs par sangs rgyas so |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{dus gsum du (in the three times) rnam par bzhugs pa'i (abiding) sangs rgyas thams cad kyang (all buddhas also), shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa la (on prajñāpāramitā) brten nas (relying), bla na med pa (unsurpassed) yang dag par rdzogs pa'i (perfect, complete) byang chub tu (to enlightenment) mngon par rdzogs par sangs rgyas so (fully awakened).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{All the buddhas who abide in the three times, relying on prajñāpāramitā, fully awaken to unsurpassed, perfect, complete enlightenment.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{23} \tib{དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པའི་སྔགས།}\\
\wylie{de lta bas na shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i sngags |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{24} \tib{རིག་པ་ཆེན་པོའི་སྔགས། བླ་ན་མེད་པའི་སྔགས། མི་མཉམ་པ་དང་མཉམ་པའི་སྔགས།}\\
\wylie{rig pa chen po'i sngags | bla na med pa'i sngags | mi mnyam pa dang mnyam pa'i sngags |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{25} \tib{སྡུག་བསྔལ་ཐམས་ཅད་རབ་ཏུ་ཞི་བར་བྱེད་པའི་སྔགས།}\\
\wylie{sdug bsngal thams cad rab tu zhi bar byed pa'i sngags |}

\vspace{0.3em}
\linenum{26} \tib{མི་རྫུན་པས་ན་བདེན་པར་ཤེས་པར་བྱ་སྟེ།}\\
\wylie{mi rdzun pas na bden par shes par bya ste |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{de lta bas na (therefore) shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i sngags (the prajñāpāramitā mantra), rig pa chen po'i sngags (the mantra of great knowledge), bla na med pa'i sngags (the unsurpassed mantra), mi mnyam pa dang mnyam pa'i sngags (the mantra equal to the unequalled), sdug bsngal thams cad rab tu zhi bar byed pa'i sngags (the mantra that completely pacifies all suffering) --- mi rdzun pas na (because it is not false) bden par (as true) shes par bya ste (it should be known).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{Therefore, the prajñāpāramitā mantra, the mantra of great knowledge, the unsurpassed mantra, the mantra equal to the unequalled, the mantra that completely pacifies all suffering, should be known as true because it is not false.}

\vspace{1em}
\linenum{27} \tib{ཤེས་རབ་ཀྱི་ཕ་རོལ་ཏུ་ཕྱིན་པའི་སྔགས་སྨྲས་པ།}\\
\wylie{shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i sngags smras pa |}

\vspace{0.5em}
\begin{center}
{\Large\tib{ཏདྱ་ཐཱ། ཨོཾ་ག་ཏེ་ག་ཏེ་པཱ་ར་ག་ཏེ་པཱ་ར་སཾ་ག་ཏེ་བོ་དྷི་སྭཱ་ཧཱ།}}\footnote{\lemma{\tib{ཨོཾ}} \textit{oṃ}] The mantra begins with oṃ in all Tibetan editions. This is a Tantric addition (9th--10th century) absent from the earliest Chinese and Sanskrit witnesses. The Tibetan was translated from a Sanskrit text that already contained this addition.}

\vspace{0.3em}
\wylie{tad+ya thA | oM ga te ga te pA ra ga te pA ra saM ga te bo d+hi swA hA |}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.5em}
\gloss{shes rab kyi pha rol tu phyin pa'i sngags smras pa (the prajñāpāramitā mantra is proclaimed): tadyathā (it runs thus): oṃ gate gate (gone, gone) pāragate (gone beyond) pārasaṃgate (gone completely beyond) bodhi (awakening) svāhā (hail!).}

\vspace{0.3em}
\fluent{The prajñāpāramitā mantra is proclaimed. It runs thus: Oṃ --- gone, gone, gone beyond, gone completely beyond --- awakening! Hail!}

\section*{Kangyur Variant Readings}

The six major Kangyur editions show minimal substantive variation. Key differences are orthographic:

\begin{itemize}
\item \wit{D Q N H}: Standard readings as printed above
\item \wit{J}: Minor spelling variants (e.g., \tib{ཀྱི}/\tib{གྱི} alternation)
\item \wit{S}: Oldest manuscript witness; occasional archaic spellings
\end{itemize}

All editions preserve:
\begin{itemize}
\item The oṃ in the mantra
\item The \textit{zad pa} (= \textit{kṣaya}) error
\item The \textit{ma thob pa} (= \textit{na-aprāptiḥ}) interpolation
\item The full frame narrative (nidāna and epilogue)
\end{itemize}

This uniformity confirms the Tibetan translation derives from a single Sanskrit source already containing these features.

\section*{Stemmatic Position}

The Tibetan Heart Sūtra occupies a specific position in the transmission:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{rcl}
Chinese T251 & $\rightarrow$ & \textit{(composition, ca. 649--656 CE)}\\
Sanskrit σ & $\rightarrow$ & \textit{(back-translation, mid-7th c.)}\\
Sanskrit σ-S & $\rightarrow$ & \textit{(short recension)}\\
Sanskrit σ-L & $\rightarrow$ & \textit{(long recension, 8th c.)}\\
\textbf{Tibetan (IOL)} & & \textit{(from σ-S, c.\ 823 CE)}\\
\textbf{Tibetan (Kangyur)} & & \textit{(from σ-L, 8th--9th c.)}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The Kangyur Tibetan is a \textbf{tertiary witness}, valuable for:
\begin{itemize}
\item Documenting the Sanskrit long recension as it existed in the 8th--9th century
\item Confirming features of the Sanskrit back-translation (e.g., \textit{kṣaya} error)
\item Showing that Tantric additions (oṃ, na-aprāptiḥ) were established by the translation date
\end{itemize}

The Dunhuang witness (IOL Tib J 751) is valuable for:
\begin{itemize}
\item Preserving the earliest datable Tibetan Heart Sūtra text
\item Attesting the short recension in Tibetan, corresponding to T251 and Hōryūji
\item Providing evidence of imperial-period translation conventions
\end{itemize}

Neither Tibetan tradition can be used to correct Chinese readings, as both derive from Sanskrit, not Chinese.
