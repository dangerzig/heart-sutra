# Falsifiability Statement and Counterevidence Checklist

## The Hypothesis

T250 and T251 are parallel compositions independently extracted from T223, rather than successive revisions of a single text.

---

## What Would Disprove This Hypothesis?

The parallel composition hypothesis makes specific predictions. The following findings would challenge or disprove it:

### 1. A Textual Intermediate

**Falsifying evidence:** Discovery of a manuscript that combines features of both T250 and T251 in a way consistent with intermediate revision -- for example, a text with T250's skandha-characteristic passage *and* T251's four epithets, or T250's 觀世音 *with* T251's 五蘊.

**Why this matters:** If revision proceeded from one text to the other, intermediate copies might preserve a transitional state. Their absence is consistent with parallel composition but not conclusive (manuscripts are lost all the time).

**Current status:** No such intermediate is known. The Dunhuang manuscripts (S.700, S.2464, P.2168, P.2323) consistently align with either the T250 or T251 tradition, not a hybrid.

### 2. Documented Deletion Practice

**Falsifying evidence:** Discovery of a Chinese Buddhist 抄經 tradition where a reviser demonstrably deleted doctrinally correct content from an earlier version.

**Why this matters:** Our argument that deletion of T250's unique passages is implausible depends on the claim that 抄經 revision practice favors expansion, not abridgment of correct doctrine. A clear counter-example would weaken this argument.

**Current status:** Ten case studies spanning five centuries document no such deletion (see CHAOJING_REVISION_PRACTICES.md). The burden of proof lies with those claiming deletion occurred.

### 3. A Common Non-T223 Source

**Falsifying evidence:** Identification of a source text (other than T223) from which both T250 and T251 derive, explaining their shared content and divergences through different levels of fidelity to that common source.

**Why this matters:** The parallel composition hypothesis locates the common source in T223. If a different common source were identified (e.g., a lost Sanskrit text, or T1509), the mechanism would change even if the "parallel" structure remained.

**Current status:** Nattier (1992) conjectured T250 might derive from T1509 (Mahaprajnaparamitopadesa), but this has not been demonstrated.

### 4. T250 Chronological Impossibility

**Falsifying evidence:** Definitive evidence that T250 was composed after T251 (e.g., T250 quoting a text composed after 656 CE, or a pre-730 CE catalogue listing T250 as dependent on T251).

**Why this matters:** If T250 is definitively later, it cannot be a parallel composition. It would instead be a backward derivation (Attwood's model).

**Current status:** The earliest attestation of T250 is the Kaiyuan Catalogue (730 CE). This is consistent with both parallel composition and later fabrication. The question remains open.

### 5. Systematic Terminology Counter-Pattern

**Falsifying evidence:** Discovery that T250 uses Xuanzang-era terminology in some passages while using Kumarajiva-era terminology in others, suggesting selective archaizing rather than genuine compositional independence.

**Why this matters:** Our passage-by-passage comparison shows T250 *consistently* uses Kumarajiva's conventions. If this consistency were broken -- if T250 used 觀自在 or 五蘊 anywhere -- it would suggest deliberate construction rather than independent composition.

**Current status:** T250's terminology is consistently pre-Xuanzang throughout. No counter-pattern has been identified.

## Counterevidence Checklist for the Chinese-Priority Framework

The parallel composition hypothesis operates within the Chinese-priority framework (Nattier 1992). The following would challenge this broader framework:

| # | Finding | Would challenge | Current status |
|---|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Sanskrit Heart Sutra manuscript dated before 649 CE | Chinese composition thesis | No such manuscript known |
| 2 | Heart Sutra passage not traceable to T223 | Extraction thesis | Core passages all trace to T223 |
| 3 | Standard PP Sanskrit (nirodha) in early Heart Sutra MS | Back-translation claim | All early MSS use kṣaya |
| 4 | Pre-Xuanzang Chinese catalogue listing T251 | Xuanzang-circle composition | No such catalogue entry |
| 5 | Independent Indian commentary on Heart Sutra pre-7th c. | Chinese origin | No such commentary known |
| 6 | T250 using post-Kumarajiva terminology | T250 compositional independence | Not found (see #5 above) |

## Addressing Confirmation Bias

The project starts from the Chinese-priority framework and frames evidence accordingly. We acknowledge this risk and address it as follows:

1. **We present data separately from interpretation.** The passage-by-passage comparison (PASSAGE_COMPARISON.md) documents factual textual differences. The parallel composition interpretation is offered as the best explanation, not the only possible one.

2. **We document what would falsify our hypothesis** (this document). A hypothesis that cannot specify its own failure conditions is unfalsifiable.

3. **We acknowledge the strongest alternative.** Attwood's model (T250 as later deliberate restoration from T223) accounts for the same textual facts. We argue parallel composition is more parsimonious, but we do not claim Attwood's model is impossible.

4. **We note limitations in our evidence base.** INBUDS, CNKI, and Siu Sai Yau's catalogue were not exhaustively searched. Fukui (1987) was not consulted in the original Japanese. We flag these gaps transparently.

5. **The systematic pattern is the key evidence.** No single observation proves parallel composition. The cumulative weight of T250 matching T223 in 8 of 10 sections, with 0 sections favoring T251, constitutes a pattern that any competing hypothesis must also explain.
