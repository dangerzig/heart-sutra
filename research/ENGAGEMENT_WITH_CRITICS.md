## Introduction

This section engages with scholars who have questioned the Chinese composition thesis on which the parallel composition hypothesis builds. While the T250/T251 question is distinct from the broader origin debate, reviewers will expect acknowledgment of contrary positions.

---

## The Chinese Composition Thesis and Its Critics

### The Nattier Thesis (1992)

Jan Nattier's landmark article argued that the Heart Sūtra was composed in Chinese and later back-translated into Sanskrit. Her evidence included:

1. **Lexical evidence**: The Sanskrit uses *kṣaya* (destruction) where standard Prajñāpāramitā uses *nirodha* (cessation). The Chinese 盡 can translate either; the back-translator chose incorrectly.

2. **Grammatical evidence**: Certain Sanskrit constructions appear to be calques from Chinese syntax.

3. **Source identification**: The Heart Sūtra's content matches Kumārajīva's T223 specifically, not other translations or Sanskrit originals.

4. **Dating**: The earliest dated witness is Chinese (Fangshan, 661 CE); the earliest Sanskrit manuscript (Hōryūji) dates to the 7th-8th century.

This thesis has gained substantial scholarly acceptance over the past three decades, including support from Attwood (2015-2024), Huifeng (2014), and others.

### Critics of the Chinese Composition Thesis

Several scholars have questioned Nattier's conclusions:

---

#### Fukui Fumimasa (福井文雅, 1934–2017)

**Position:** Fukui's central thesis, developed before Nattier's 1992 article, is that the Heart Sūtra is not a sūtra in the traditional sense but rather a **dhāraṇī** (陀羅尼) composed for liturgical purposes. The mantra at the end represents the text's true "core," with the preceding prose functioning as an extended framing. His 1987 monograph (*Hannya shingyō no rekishiteki kenkyū*) laid this groundwork; his 1994 article in *Bukkyōgaku* 36 was the first direct Japanese response to Nattier; and his 2000 *Sōgōteki kenkyū* expanded the argument. Fukui's response to Nattier has never been translated into English.

**Key arguments:**
- The *kṣaya/nirodha* argument, while suggestive, involves a single term and may be less decisive than claimed
- Historical accounts of Xuanzang receiving the text in India deserve more weight than Nattier allows
- The absence of pre-7th century Sanskrit manuscripts does not prove non-existence
- Nattier's use of "core passage" (referring to the section derived from the Large PP) conflicts with Fukui's thesis that the mantra constitutes the core
- The Heart Sūtra should be classified as a dhāraṇī, not a sūtra — this reframing affects how "apocryphal" should be understood

**Assessment:** According to Attwood's review, Fukui appears to have struggled with Nattier's English idiom and never fully engaged with her comparative-philological method (Nattier wrote an unpublished rebuttal in 1995). Nevertheless, Fukui's dhāraṇī thesis has been influential and was later developed differently by Silk (2021).

**Response for paper:** The parallel composition hypothesis does not depend on definitively resolving the Chinese-vs-Sanskrit origin debate. Whether the Heart Sūtra was originally composed in Chinese (Nattier) or translated from Sanskrit (Fukui), the *relationship between T250 and T251* can still be assessed on independent grounds: the character-for-character T223 matches, the consistent terminology differences, and the presence of material in T250 absent from T251. Fukui's dhāraṇī thesis is noted in our methodology as a significant alternative classification.

---

#### Harada Waso (原田和宗, b. 1959)

**Position:** Harada's 2010 monograph (*'Hannya shingyō' seiritsu shiron*, 446 pp.) is the most extensive Japanese-language work opposing the Chinese-origins hypothesis. His method involves comparing every word and phrase against sūtras, treatises (*śāstra*), and biographical texts (*avadāna*) to recover the original meaning and reconstruct the author's intentions. His earlier 2002 article in *Mikkyō bunka* provided an annotated Japanese translation of the Sanskrit short recension.

**Key arguments:**
- The Sanskrit Heart Sūtra reads as fluent Sanskrit composition, not back-translation, which typically produces awkward constructions
- The text fits naturally into Indian Prajñāpāramitā traditions
- Avalokiteśvara's presence as the main speaker is justifiable given the documented blending of Prajñāpāramitā and Avalokiteśvara traditions in Indian Buddhism from at least Faxian's time (4th–5th c. CE); the Aṣṭasāhasrikā already has speakers other than the Buddha (Subhūti, Śāriputra, Ānanda)
- Evidence from Kuiji's 7th-century commentary (T1710) and Wŏnch'ŭk's commentary (T1711) supports an Indian origin
- The text represents a genuine crossroads between Mahāyāna and Esoteric (tantric) Buddhism

**Response for paper:** Even accepting Harada's position that the Sanskrit is an original composition, this does not address the T250/T251 relationship. T250 and T251 both exist as Chinese texts with documented differences. The question of their relationship is independent of whether either was translated from Sanskrit. Harada's argument about Avalokiteśvara's precedent in the Aṣṭasāhasrikā is noted as a substantive counter-point to Nattier.

---

#### Ishii Kōsei (石井公成)

**Position:** Ishii's 2015 article in *Indogaku Bukkyōgaku kenkyū* (translated into English by Jeffrey Kotyk) is the most focused Japanese-language critique directly targeting Nattier's specific claim that Xuanzang composed the Heart Sūtra. His 2021 *Acta Asiatica* article examines what Wŏnch'ŭk's near-contemporary commentary reveals about the Chinese and Sanskrit texts available to him. Ishii does not dismiss the Chinese-origins thesis wholesale but challenges the specific mechanism Nattier proposed (Xuanzang as compiler/back-translator).

**Key arguments:**
- Nattier's linguistic evidence is more ambiguous than presented; alternative explanations exist for the apparent back-translation features
- Evidence from Kuiji's and Wŏnch'ŭk's near-contemporary commentaries undermines the claim that Xuanzang fabricated the text — Wŏnch'ŭk's commentary presupposes access to Sanskrit materials that complicate the Chinese-origins thesis
- The 7th-century Chinese context of Heart Sūtra circulation deserves more attention, but this does not necessarily imply Chinese authorship
- Ishii acknowledges the Chinese context matters while defending the text's Indian origins

**Response for paper:** The parallel composition hypothesis actually aligns with Ishii's call for attention to the 7th-century Chinese context. By examining how T250 and T251 each relate to T223, we are doing precisely this kind of detailed contextual analysis. The hypothesis does not require accepting every detail of Nattier's argument. Ishii's emphasis on the commentary evidence is noted; our edition documents Wŏnch'ŭk's variant readings where they are relevant to the textual apparatus.

---

#### Dan Lusthaus

**Position:** Has raised objections based on 7th-century commentaries by Kuiji and Woncheuk.

**Key arguments:**
- Kuiji's commentary (T1710) and Woncheuk's commentary (T1711) both treat the Heart Sūtra as an Indian text translated by Xuanzang
- These near-contemporary sources should not be dismissed

**Response for paper:** Woncheuk's commentary is actually relevant to our argument. Woncheuk notes variant readings between Heart Sūtra versions, including the 苦惱 variant we document in T250. This suggests that even in the 7th century, scholars were aware of different Heart Sūtra textual traditions. The parallel composition hypothesis provides a framework for understanding why such variants existed.

---

#### Hyun Choo

**Position:** Has questioned the dating evidence and the dismissal of traditional accounts.

**Key arguments:**
- Traditional accounts of Xuanzang receiving the Heart Sūtra should be taken seriously
- The dating argument is less conclusive than claimed

**Response for paper:** The parallel composition hypothesis is actually compatible with Xuanzang composing T251. If Xuanzang composed T251 (as Nattier suggests) and T250 represents an earlier independent tradition, our hypothesis holds. If Xuanzang translated T251 from Sanskrit (as Hyun Choo prefers), T250 could still represent a parallel Chinese tradition. The hypothesis addresses the T250/T251 relationship specifically, not the ultimate origin question.

---

#### Ji Yun (紀贇)

**Position:** Ji Yun (Singapore Buddhist College) offers the most nuanced Chinese-language engagement with Nattier's thesis. His 2012 article in *Fùyán Fóxué yánjiū* (English translation by Chin Shih-Foong in *Singapore Journal of Buddhist Studies* 4, 2017) partially accepts and partially rejects the Chinese-origins thesis.

**Key arguments:**
- The Heart Sūtra is a **chāo jīng** (抄經, "digest text" or "condensed/extracted sūtra") rather than an apocryphal text in the traditional sense
- The text's lack of conventional sūtra elements (proper introduction, conclusion with assembly dispersal) supports its classification as a dhāraṇī
- Its compositional method (extraction from the Large PP) is consistent with a well-known Chinese genre of "digest texts"
- Avalokiteśvara's role reflects tantric influence around 650 CE
- The term "apocryphal" (偽經) is misleading — the text is better understood as a genre-specific product of Chinese Buddhist practice, not a deliberate forgery

**Response for paper:** Ji Yun's *chāo jīng* reframing is directly relevant to our analysis of T250/T251 as parallel extraction efforts. Our `research/CHAOJING_REVISION_PRACTICES.md` documents this genre and its conventions. Ji Yun's insight that the Heart Sūtra is neither "authentic sūtra" nor "apocryphal forgery" but a recognized genre of digest text supports our treatment of T250 and T251 as products of a specific compositional practice rather than as revisions of one another.

---

#### Siu Sai Yau (蕭世友)

**Position:** Siu (Hang Seng University of Hong Kong) has conducted cross-philological study of Chinese and Sanskrit Heart Sūtra texts alongside other medieval-period Sanskrit Mahāyāna sūtras. His 2019 comprehensive bibliographic catalogue (1912–2013) is an invaluable reference work.

**Key arguments:**
- The Heart Sūtra could not have been composed in China but was composed in India
- Comparison with extant Sanskrit Buddhist manuscript fragments supports an Indian compositional origin
- Proposes that a Sanskrit Heart Sūtra was possibly transmitted from East India to Sichuan during the Sui Dynasty (581–618) and translated into Chinese in the early Tang
- The shorter Heart Sūtra is a "hybrid text of mantra and dhāraṇī," a core text type within Prajñāpāramitā literature

**Response for paper:** Siu's argument for an Indian Sanskrit precursor is noted. Regardless of the original language of composition, the T250/T251 relationship can be assessed on independent Chinese-textual grounds. His bibliographic catalogue is cited as a comprehensive survey of the field.

---

#### The *Acta Asiatica* 121 (2021) Special Issue

This volume, published by the Tōhō Gakkai (Institute of Eastern Culture, Tokyo), is the single most important recent publication assembling Japanese and international scholarship on the Heart Sūtra. It contains articles by Saitō Akira (on Avalokiteśvara's role), Watanabe Shōgo (on the Prajñāpāramitā lineage), Horiuchi Toshio (on Indian commentaries), Ishii Kōsei (on Wŏnch'ŭk's evidence), and Jonathan Silk (on the Heart Sūtra as dhāraṇī). The contributors are largely sympathetic to the Indian-origin thesis, though their arguments are scholarly and evidence-based. Silk's article is particularly notable: he argues the Heart Sūtra was, in certain contexts, treated as a whole as a dhāraṇī, not merely a sūtra containing a dhāraṇī — connecting to Fukui's earlier thesis but approaching it from reception history rather than origin claims.

**Response for paper:** We engage with individual articles from this collection where relevant (see entries above). The volume as a whole represents the most authoritative recent collective statement from scholars who question or modify the Chinese-origins thesis. We acknowledge these positions while noting that the parallel composition hypothesis addresses a narrower question (the T250/T251 relationship) that does not depend on resolving the broader origin debate.

## Position Statement for the Paper

The following paragraph can be adapted for the paper's Introduction or Discussion section:

> **Scholarly Context.** This paper builds on Jan Nattier's (1992) analysis of the Heart Sūtra as a Chinese composition, a thesis that has gained substantial scholarly support (Attwood 2015–2024, Huifeng 2014) while remaining debated. Scholars including Fukui Fumimasa (1987, 1994, 2000), Harada Waso (2002, 2010), Ishii Kōsei (2015, 2021), Siu Sai Yau (2017), and Dan Lusthaus have raised objections based on linguistic interpretation, historical accounts, and 7th-century commentarial evidence. Ji Yun (2012/2017) offers a nuanced middle position, accepting Chinese composition but reframing the text as a *chāo jīng* (digest text) rather than an apocryphal work. The *Acta Asiatica* 121 (2021) special issue assembles the most recent collective scholarship on this question. We acknowledge that the Chinese-vs-Sanskrit origin question remains contested, and that our engagement with Chinese- and Japanese-language scholarship is necessarily partial (see Limitations below).
>
> The case for Chinese compositional priority has been significantly strengthened by Attwood's comprehensive section-by-section comparison of the Chinese and Sanskrit texts (2023), which identifies numerous systematic differences arising from the back-translation process, and by his revised editions of both texts (2024), which for the first time produce fully parsable versions in both languages.
>
> However, the parallel composition hypothesis advanced here does not require definitively resolving this broader debate. Whether the Heart Sūtra was originally composed in Chinese or translated from Sanskrit, the relationship between T250 and T251—two Chinese short-recension texts with documented differences—can be assessed on independent grounds. The evidence we present (systematic terminology differences, T223 extraction patterns, unique T250 passages) bears on the T250/T251 relationship specifically. Our hypothesis is compatible with either position on the ultimate origin question: it proposes that T250 and T251 represent parallel compositional efforts, not successive revisions, regardless of whether either derived from an earlier Sanskrit source.
>
> **Limitations.** Our engagement with the Chinese- and Japanese-language scholarly literature is partial, relying on published English translations (Ji Yun 2017, Ishii/Kotyk 2015, Kudō & Fukita 2006), English-language review articles (particularly Attwood's review of *Acta Asiatica* 121 in *Buddhist Studies Review* 39.2, 2023), bilingual publications, and secondary summaries. Key works—including Fukui's 1994 rebuttal, Harada's 2010 monograph, and Watanabe's 2009 comprehensive study—have been consulted through secondary literature and partial translations. A complete assessment of CJK-language scholarship would require specialist linguistic competence that this edition does not claim. We cite Siu Sai Yau's bibliographic catalogue (2019) as the most comprehensive survey of the field (covering 1912–2013) for readers seeking fuller coverage.

## Key Point for Reviewers

The parallel composition hypothesis is *more modest* than the Chinese composition thesis. We are not arguing that the Heart Sūtra was definitively composed in Chinese. We are arguing that the two Chinese short-recension texts (T250 and T251) show evidence of parallel composition rather than linear revision.

This claim can be evaluated on its own terms:
- Does T250 consistently match T223 where T251 innovates? (Yes)
- Does T250 contain material absent from T251? (Yes)
- Would a reviser delete coherent doctrinal elaborations? (Unlikely based on 抄經 practice)
- Are the terminology patterns consistent with revision? (No—they are systematic and incompatible)

These questions can be answered without resolving the broader origin debate.
