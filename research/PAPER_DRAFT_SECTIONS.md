## A. Scholarly Context (TODO #2: Critic Engagement)

### For Introduction (condensed from ENGAGEMENT_WITH_CRITICS.md + literature review)

This paper builds on Jan Nattier's (1992) analysis of the Heart Sutra as a Chinese composition extracted from Kumarajiva's Large Prajnaparamita (T223), a thesis that has gained substantial scholarly support (Attwood 2015-2024; Huifeng 2014; McRae 1988; Watanabe 1990, 1991) while remaining contested. Fukui Fumimasa (1987, 1994), Harada Waso (2002, 2010), Ishii Kosei (2021), and Siu Sai Yau (2017) have defended Indian Sanskrit composition on various grounds. Dan Lusthaus and Hyun Choo have raised objections based on 7th-century commentarial evidence and traditional accounts respectively. We acknowledge that the Chinese-vs-Sanskrit origin question remains debated.

However, the hypothesis advanced here does not require resolving this broader debate. We propose that T250 (the Damingzhoujing) and T251 (the Xinjing) represent parallel compositions independently extracted from T223, rather than successive revisions. This claim can be evaluated on its own terms, regardless of the ultimate origin question.

Our hypothesis engages most directly with the current critical consensus, represented by Attwood (2020), that T250 is a later fabrication deliberately crafted to authenticate T251. Attwood argues that "lines excised from the quoted passage during the creation of the Xinjing were restored directly from the source -- Kumarajiva's Large Sutra (T223) -- when the Damingzhoujing was created at a later date." The textual facts we document are the same facts Attwood documents: T250 matches T223 closely while T251 innovates. Our disagreement is interpretive, not factual. We argue that parallel composition provides a more parsimonious account of the systematic consistency of T250's T223-fidelity across all sections examined.

Watanabe Shogo's (1991) examination of the relationship between the Damingzhoujing and the Dapinjing (大品般若経) is the most directly relevant prior study. His conclusion -- that T250 is a "spurious scripture" (偽経) -- differs from ours, but his textual observations support the same key finding: T250's content derives from the Large Sutra tradition. To our knowledge, the hypothesis that T250 and T251 represent parallel compositions independently extracted from T223 has not been previously proposed in the published literature, though we note that INBUDS, CNKI, and Siu Sai Yau's comprehensive catalogue (1912-2013) could not be exhaustively searched.

### For Discussion (Attwood response)

The primary alternative to our hypothesis is Attwood's (2020) model, in which T250 was deliberately constructed after T251 by a forger who restored T223 material to create the appearance of an earlier translation. This model accounts for the same textual facts we document. However, it requires the forger to have:

(a) Systematically replaced every Xuanzang-convention term with its Kumarajiva equivalent (觀自在→觀世音, 五蘊→五陰, 舍利子→舍利弗, 菩提薩埵→菩薩, 般若波羅蜜多→般若波羅蜜) without a single lapse;

(b) Added two complete, doctrinally coherent passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation) drawn from the Large Prajnaparamita tradition;

(c) Reduced the four epithets to three, matching the Large Sutra pattern exactly, with consistent 明呪 terminology throughout;

(d) Made numerous small adjustments to phrasing (非色異空 for 色不異空; 亦如是 for 亦復如是; 離 for 遠離) that consistently reproduce Kumarajiva's translation style.

While such sophistication is not impossible, parallel composition offers a simpler explanation: two compilers working independently from T223, one using Kumarajiva-era conventions and one using Xuanzang-era conventions, would naturally produce exactly this pattern.

## B. Terminological Definitions (TODO #11)

### For Methodology Section

We define key terms as follows:

**Digest text** (抄經, chaojing): A condensed scripture compiled by selecting and arranging passages from one or more existing Buddhist translations. The Heart Sutra is a digest text extracted primarily from Kumarajiva's Large Prajnaparamita (T223). Attwood notes that "hundreds of similar texts were composed in China" (2020). The term is used descriptively and does not imply judgement about authenticity.

**Compositionally prior:** Text A is compositionally prior to Text B when B's content can be shown to derive from A's content -- whether by extraction, translation, paraphrase, or other compositional process. This is established by specific textual evidence (shared wording, characteristic errors, terminology patterns), not by assumed chronology. A text may be compositionally prior even if its surviving manuscripts are later.

**Derived tradition:** A textual tradition that originated through transformation of another text. In this paper, we follow Nattier (1992) in treating the Sanskrit Heart Sutra as derived from the Chinese via back-translation. "Derived" describes a compositional relationship, not a value judgement.

**Back-translation:** The process of translating a text back into its source language (or a presumed source language) from a translation. The Sanskrit Heart Sutra appears to be a back-translation from Chinese, as evidenced by the use of kṣaya where standard Prajnaparamita Sanskrit uses nirodha -- both translatable by the Chinese 盡 (Nattier 1992: 167-169).

**Parallel composition:** The hypothesis that two texts were composed independently from a shared source, rather than one being derived from the other. We propose T250 and T251 as parallel compositions from T223. This is distinguished from "independent translation" (both translated from the same foreign-language source) by the fact that the shared source (T223) is itself Chinese.

**Note on circularity:** We are careful to distinguish the evidence for compositional priority (specific textual features like kṣaya/nirodha, T223 extraction patterns) from the conclusion (Chinese priority, parallel composition). The evidence does not presuppose the conclusion; rather, the conclusion is offered as the best explanation of the evidence.

## C. Additional Scholarly Authorities (TODO #12)

### Scholars Beyond Nattier and Attwood Who Support or Engage with the Evidence

To reduce over-reliance on Nattier and Attwood as primary authorities, we cite the following independent scholars:

1. **Watanabe Shogo** (渡辺章悟): His 1991 examination of the Damingzhoujing-Dapinjing relationship independently confirms that T250's content derives from the Large Sutra tradition. His 2021 survey in *Acta Asiatica* provides the most comprehensive overview of Heart Sutra scholarship.

2. **Huifeng** (Shi Huifeng, 2014): His textual criticism of the Prajnaparamita tradition ("Apocryphal Treatment for Conze's Heart Problems") provides independent corrections to Conze's edition and identifies the na-apraptiḥ interpolation, supporting the view that the text evolved through accretion.

3. **Silk, Jonathan** (2021): His analysis of the Heart Sutra as dharani in *Acta Asiatica* provides an independent framework for understanding the text's liturgical function, compatible with the digest-text model.

4. **Funayama Toru** (船山徹): His work on the boundary "between Chinese translation and Chinese composition" (2013) provides the theoretical framework for understanding how Chinese monks adopted Buddhist translation style in their own compositions -- precisely the compositional practice behind 抄經 texts.

5. **Storch, Tanya** (2014): Her *History of Chinese Buddhist Bibliography* documents Sengyou's critical stance on 抄經 texts, providing institutional context for digest-text composition practices.

6. **Tokuno, Kyoko** (1990): Her study of indigenous scripture evaluation in Chinese Buddhist catalogues provides the bibliographic framework within which T250 and T251 were classified and transmitted.

7. **Buswell, Robert** (1990): His foundational *Chinese Buddhist Apocrypha* provides the scholarly framework for understanding composite Chinese Buddhist texts and their relationship to Indian sources.

8. **Woncheuk** (圓測, 613-696 CE): His commentary on the Heart Sutra (T1711) is a primary source that attests to variant readings between Heart Sutra versions already in the 7th century, including the 苦惱 variant we document.
