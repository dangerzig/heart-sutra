## Critical Dating Sensitivity: T250

The date of T250 is the single most important dating question for the parallel composition hypothesis.

### If T250 is genuinely early (pre-Xuanzang):
- **Supports parallel composition.** Two independent compilers working from T223 at different times, using the translation conventions of their respective eras.
- The pre-Xuanzang terminology (觀世音, 五陰, 舍利弗, 明呪) is naturally explained.
- The three-epithet pattern matching T223 is naturally explained.

### If T250 is later than T251:
- **Does not disprove parallel composition.** Two compilers could work independently even if temporally close.
- **However**, it opens the alternative hypothesis (Attwood 2020) that T250 was deliberately constructed to authenticate T251 by restoring T223 material.
- The consistency of pre-Xuanzang terminology becomes harder to explain as natural and easier to explain as deliberate archaism.

### What evidence could resolve this:
1. **A securely dated T250-type manuscript** (e.g., a Dunhuang manuscript with colophon date before 649 CE)
2. **A pre-Xuanzang catalogue reference** to a short Heart Sutra with T250-type title
3. **Compositional analysis** showing T250's phrasing is inconsistent with deliberate archaism (our current approach)

### Our position:
The parallel composition hypothesis does not depend on T250 being early. Our argument is structural: the systematic consistency of T250's T223-fidelity across all sections is better explained by independent extraction than by deliberate restoration. This structural argument holds regardless of T250's date, because a forger's task (replacing every Xuanzang-convention term with its Kumārajīva equivalent, adding two doctrinally coherent passages, reducing four epithets to three) remains improbable whether the forger worked in the 7th century or the 10th.

## Dating Evidence Types and Their Reliability

### Most reliable: Physical inscription with colophon
- **Fangshan Stele (661 CE)**: Date carved in stone. Cannot be later than stated; could theoretically be earlier (if the inscription commemorates a copy, not the original carving). But epigraphic analysis confirms Tang dynasty carving style.

### Highly reliable: Manuscript colophon
- **P.2323 (851 CE)**: Scribe's date on the manuscript. The manuscript itself is the dated object. The text it copies may be older.

### Reliable: Historical document reference
- **Kotyk's 656 CE letter**: The letter is a well-attested historical document. The object it refers to (a gold-lettered Heart Sutra) has not survived, but the reference is reliable.

### Moderately reliable: Translator records
- **T257 (1005 CE)**: Song dynasty translation bureau records are generally reliable for Dānapāla's translations. The date of translation is secure; the date of the Sanskrit source manuscript is unknown.

### Moderately reliable: Paleographic dating
- **Dunhuang manuscripts without colophons**: Dated by handwriting style, paper type, and associated texts. Typically reliable to within ±100 years. S.700 (7th–8th c.) as a T250-type manuscript places the tradition within this window.

### Low reliability: Catalogue attribution
- **T250 attribution to Kumārajīva (402–412 CE)**: The Kaiyuan Catalogue (730 CE) makes this attribution, but it is over 300 years after the alleged translation. Buddhist bibliographers frequently attributed anonymous texts to famous translators. The attribution cannot be taken at face value.
