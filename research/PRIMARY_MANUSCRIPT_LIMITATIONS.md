## What Has NOT Been Consulted

### Physical manuscripts not examined

1. **Dunhuang originals** — S.2464, S.700, P.2168, P.2192, P.3917, P.2323, S.4289 are cataloged but not personally inspected. IDP (International Dunhuang Project) provides digital images for some; diplomatic transcriptions would require paleographic expertise in Chinese manuscript hands.

2. **Hōryū-ji palm-leaf manuscript** — The oldest known Sanskrit witness. Published readings exist (Müller 1884) but the manuscript itself is held in the Hōryū-ji temple archive with restricted access.

3. **Gilgit fragments** — Sanskrit manuscript fragments from Gilgit, Pakistan. Published by Conze but not personally examined.

4. **Fangshan stone inscription** — Physically located at Yunju Temple, Beijing. Published transcriptions and rubbings exist; direct inspection would confirm readings.

5. **Nara-period 写経 (shakyō) copies** — Japanese temple archives (Tōdai-ji, Kōya-san, Shōsōin) may preserve early T250-type or T251-type readings. The Shōsōin digitization project may make some accessible in future.

6. **Nepalese manuscripts** — Held in various Nepalese and European collections. Published collations exist but vary in quality.

## Impact on Claims

### Claims that DO NOT depend on primary manuscript access

1. **T250 matches T223 more closely than T251 does** — This is based on comparing published Taishō texts. Since both T250 and T223 are from the same Taishō Canon, the comparison is internally consistent.

2. **Systematic terminological differences between T250 and T251** — Observable in any reliable printed edition.

3. **Sanskrit shows back-translation indicators** — Identified from published IAST transcriptions following Nattier (1992).

4. **Parallel composition hypothesis** — The argument is structural and does not depend on variant readings from specific manuscripts.

### Claims that WOULD benefit from primary manuscript access

1. **Textual stability of T251 tradition** — Dunhuang manuscript collation would either confirm or challenge the stability of the received text.

2. **Early attestation of T250-type readings** — S.700 (Dunhuang) reportedly preserves T250-type text. Diplomatic transcription would strengthen the case for early T250 circulation.

3. **Stemmatic relationships** — True stemmatic analysis requires consulting primary witnesses, not editions already filtered through editorial decisions.

4. **Variant classification** — Some orthographic variants visible in manuscripts (ligature forms, character variants) are normalized in printed editions.

## Honest Assessment

This edition is best described as a **critical analysis of published editions** rather than a **primary-source critical edition**. This is an important distinction.

**What we can claim:** Our analysis of the compositional relationships between T250, T251, and T223 is sound because it rests on textual content that is stable across all known witnesses. The parallel composition hypothesis concerns the macro-level relationship between these texts, not micro-level variant readings.

**What we cannot claim:** We cannot claim to have produced a definitive critical text of any individual witness, since we have not collated primary manuscripts.

## Path Forward

For the paper:
- Acknowledge this limitation explicitly in the Methodology section
- Frame the work as "analysis of the received textual tradition" rather than "critical edition"
- Note that primary manuscript collation is a desideratum for the full edition

For the edition (future work):
1. Obtain IDP digital images of Dunhuang Heart Sutra manuscripts
2. Produce diplomatic transcriptions of S.2464, S.700, P.2168
3. Request access to Hōryū-ji manuscript images (or use published photographs)
4. Collate Nepalese manuscripts from published photographs where available
5. Document all collation steps with images and diplomatic transcriptions
