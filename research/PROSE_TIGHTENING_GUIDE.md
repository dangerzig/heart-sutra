## 5. Structural Recommendation

The research documents recommend this structure (from `research/INTERPRETIVE_DESCRIPTIVE.md`):

1. **Introduction** — Context, literature review (condensed), hypothesis statement
2. **Evidence** — 6 descriptive findings (tables where possible, minimal narration)
3. **Analysis** — Variant classification, passage comparison, terminological analysis
4. **Argument** — Parallel composition hypothesis, Attwood response, falsifiability
5. **Limitations** — Manuscript access, dating sensitivity, untested predictions
6. **Conclusion** — Summary, implications, future work (including māyā→śūnyatā note)

This structure naturally avoids repetition because evidence is presented once, interpreted once, and argued once.

## 6. Style Notes

- Prefer active voice for interpretive claims: "We propose" not "It has been proposed"
- Prefer "Text X shows Y" over "It can be observed that Text X appears to show Y"
- Remove hedging language from well-established facts (Nattier's extraction thesis, the terminological differences)
- Reserve hedging for genuinely uncertain claims (T250's date, the parallel composition hypothesis itself)
- Use footnotes for scholarly apparatus, not parenthetical asides in the main text
