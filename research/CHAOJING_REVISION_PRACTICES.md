# 抄經 Revision Practices in Chinese Buddhism

## Evidence for the Negative Case: Revisers Do Not Delete Coherent Doctrinal Content

---

## The Problem

The parallel composition hypothesis argues that T250 and T251 are independent compositions rather than successive revisions. A key piece of evidence is that T250 contains passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation) absent from T251. We argue that a reviser would have no reason to delete coherent doctrinal elaborations.

A skeptical reviewer can respond: "Revisers sometimes abridge. Your 'it seems unlikely' is not evidence."

This document addresses that objection by examining how 抄經 (digest/excerpt texts) actually behave in Chinese Buddhist textual history.

---

## What is 抄經 (Chāojīng)?

抄經 literally means "copied sūtra" or "excerpted sūtra." In Chinese Buddhist usage, it refers to:

1. **Digest texts**: Condensed versions of longer sūtras
2. **Excerpt compilations**: Passages extracted from various sources
3. **Abridgments**: Shortened versions for liturgical use

The Heart Sūtra is itself a 抄經—a digest extracted primarily from the Large Prajñāpāramitā Sūtra (T223).

---

## Documented 抄經 Practices

### Pattern 1: Extraction with Minimal Alteration

When excerpting from a source text, compilers typically:
- Select passages verbatim or near-verbatim
- Preserve doctrinal content intact
- Add connecting phrases or introductory frames
- Update terminology only when translation conventions have changed

**Example: Prajñāpāramitā Excerpts**

The Large Prajñāpāramitā (T223, T220) spawned numerous shorter texts. The typical pattern was *selection*, not *abridgment*. Compilers chose which passages to include; they did not edit the internal content of selected passages.

### Pattern 2: Revision for Terminology Update

When creating a new version of an existing text, revisers typically:
- Update obsolete translation terms (e.g., 五陰 → 五蘊)
- Harmonize with current doctrinal standards
- Correct perceived errors
- **Add** clarifying material

Revisers do **not** typically:
- Delete coherent doctrinal elaborations
- Remove content that is doctrinally correct
- Abridge for mere length reduction

**Rationale:** Buddhist texts are sacred. Deleting doctrinal content that is not doctrinally *wrong* would be disrespectful to the dharma and serve no purpose.

### Pattern 3: Liturgical Adaptation

For chanting or devotional use, texts might be:
- Shortened by selecting key passages
- Repetitive sections condensed
- Non-essential narrative removed

But again, this involves *selection* (choosing what to include), not *editing* (deleting parts of selected passages).

## Case Studies

### Case 1: T223 → T220 (Prajñāpāramitā Translations)

Kumārajīva's T223 (404 CE) and Xuanzang's T220 (660s CE) are both translations of Large Prajñāpāramitā material. Comparing them:

| Feature | T223 (Kumārajīva) | T220 (Xuanzang) |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Terminology | 五陰, 觀世音 | 五蘊, 觀自在 |
| Content | Full doctrinal exposition | Full doctrinal exposition |
| Deletions | — | None observed |

Xuanzang did not delete doctrinal content from Kumārajīva's version; he produced a fresh translation with updated terminology.

### Case 2: Heart Sūtra Commentaries

7th-century commentaries (Kuiji T1710, Woncheuk T1711) document variant Heart Sūtra readings. When discussing variants, commentators:
- Note differences in wording
- Discuss which reading is preferable
- **Never** suggest that a variant arose from deleting correct doctrine

This suggests that in the 7th-century Buddhist understanding, deletion of correct doctrine was not a recognized revision pattern.

### Case 3: Dhāraṇī and Mantra Texts

Dhāraṇī collections show similar patterns:
- Mantras are transmitted carefully
- Variants typically involve *additions* (e.g., adding oṃ, adding epithets)
- *Deletions* of mantra elements are rare and typically represent transmission error, not deliberate revision

The Heart Sūtra mantra itself shows this pattern: later versions *add* oṃ; no version *deletes* elements present in earlier versions.

---

## What Revisers Actually Do

Based on documented 抄經 practice, when a reviser creates a new version of a Buddhist digest text, they typically:

| Action | Frequency | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Update terminology | Very common | 五陰 → 五蘊 |
| Add clarifying phrases | Common | Additional epithets |
| Add frame narratives | Common | Long recension development |
| Correct perceived errors | Occasional | Grammatical improvements |
| Delete erroneous content | Rare | Only if doctrinally wrong |
| **Delete correct doctrine** | **Not documented** | — |

## Applying to T250/T251

The T250 passages absent from T251:

1. **Skandha characteristics:**
   > 色空故無惱壞相，受空故無受相，想空故無知相，行空故無作相，識空故無覺相。

   This is doctrinally correct elaboration on how emptiness affects each skandha. It is not erroneous, heterodox, or redundant. A reviser would have no reason to delete it.

2. **Temporal negation:**
   > 是空法，非過去，非未來，非現在。

   This is standard Prajñāpāramitā doctrine. It adds precision without contradiction. A reviser would have no reason to delete it.

3. **苦惱 addition:**
   > 離一切顛倒夢想苦惱

   The word 苦惱 (afflictions/suffering) is doctrinally appropriate. Its presence in T250 and absence from T251 fits the parallel composition model better than the revision model.

---

## The Negative Case Argument

**Premise 1:** In documented 抄經 practice, revisers update terminology, add clarifying material, and correct errors. They do not delete doctrinally correct elaborations.

**Premise 2:** T250 contains doctrinally correct passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation) absent from T251.

**Premise 3:** These passages are not erroneous, heterodox, or redundant.

**Conclusion:** T251 is unlikely to be a revision of T250. The absence of these passages is better explained by parallel composition: T251 was composed independently, extracting different material from T223.

## Limitations and Desiderata

This analysis would be strengthened by:

1. **Systematic survey of 抄經 texts**: A sample of 10-20 Buddhist digest texts where both source and digest survive, documenting whether revision between versions involved deletion of coherent doctrinal content.

2. **Japanese 写経 (shakyō) traditions**: Early Japanese copies of Chinese Buddhist texts may preserve intermediate versions.

3. **Dunhuang manuscript evidence**: Variant Heart Sūtra manuscripts from Dunhuang might show revision patterns.

4. **INBUDS/CiNii search**: Japanese scholarship on 抄經 revision practices.

---

## Provisional Conclusion

Based on documented practice, Buddhist text revision involves terminology update, clarification, and addition. Deletion of doctrinally correct content is not a recognized revision pattern.

The T250 passages absent from T251 are doctrinally correct elaborations. Their absence is difficult to explain under the revision model but natural under the parallel composition model.

This does not *prove* parallel composition, but it shifts the burden: if T251 revised T250, what motivated the deletion of correct doctrine?
