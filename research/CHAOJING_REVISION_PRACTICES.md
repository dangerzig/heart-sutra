# 抄經 Revision Practices in Chinese Buddhism

## Evidence for the Negative Case: Revisers Do Not Delete Coherent Doctrinal Content

---

## The Problem

The parallel composition hypothesis argues that T250 and T251 are independent compositions rather than successive revisions. A key piece of evidence is that T250 contains passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation) absent from T251. We argue that a reviser would have no reason to delete coherent doctrinal elaborations.

A skeptical reviewer can respond: "Revisers sometimes abridge. Your 'it seems unlikely' is not evidence."

This document addresses that objection by examining how 抄經 (digest/excerpt texts) actually behave in Chinese Buddhist textual history, drawing on web-based research conducted in February 2026.

---

## Part I: Defining the 抄經 Tradition

### 1.1 What is 抄經 (Chāojīng)?

抄經 literally means "copied sūtra" or "excerpted sūtra." According to the Wisdom Library definition, 抄經 refers to a "condensed scripture"--a text compiled from parts extracted from a scripture within Buddhism. In Chinese Buddhist usage, the term covers several related practices:

1. **Digest texts** (chāojīng proper): Condensed versions of longer sūtras, composed by selecting and arranging passages from a source text to convey its essence. "Hundreds of similar texts were composed in China, where they are known as 'digest texts' (Chin, chāo jīng), but only the Heart Sutra was translated back into Sanskrit" (Attwood, *Tricycle*).

2. **Excerpt compilations**: Passages extracted from one or multiple sources and assembled into a new composition. The Heart Sūtra is a prime example: a collection of reused passages, largely from Kumārajīva's Dàjīng (T223), with a dhāraṇī likely copied from Atikūṭa's Tuóluóniji jīng (T901).

3. **Composite apocryphal texts**: More elaborate compositions that integrate narrative and doctrinal material from several sūtras. The *Sūtra on Transforming the Female Form* (轉女身經, *Zhuan nüshen jing*) is a documented example (Balkwill 2016).

4. **Abridgments for liturgical use**: Shortened versions selected for chanting or devotional practice.

### 1.2 How 抄經 Differs from Other Textual Activities

| Activity | Chinese | Description | Relationship to Source |
|----------|---------|-------------|----------------------|
| Translation | 翻譯 (fānyì) | Rendering Indic-language text into Chinese | Aims for correspondence with foreign original |
| Commentary | 注疏 (zhùshū) | Explanatory apparatus on a base text | Supplements without replacing |
| 抄經 (digest) | 抄經 (chāojīng) | Extracting/condensing passages from source | Selects from existing Chinese translations |
| Abridgement | 略 (lüè) | Shortening by compression | Reduces length while preserving structure |
| Apocryphal composition | 偽經 (wěijīng) | Entirely new text in sūtra form | Creates new content in Indian style |

The distinction between 抄經 and 翻譯 is critical: a digest composer works from an existing Chinese translation, selecting passages; a translator works from a foreign-language manuscript. The Heart Sūtra is a 抄經 from T223, not a translation from Sanskrit (Nattier 1992).

The distinction between 抄經 and 略 is also important: abridgement (略) compresses an existing text's *structure*; 抄經 *selects* passages from a source to create a thematically focused new composition. In 抄經, the composer chooses *what to include*; the decision about exclusion operates at the level of passage selection, not internal editing of chosen passages.

### 1.3 Cataloguers' Treatment of 抄經

The Chinese Buddhist bibliographic tradition (經錄, jīnglù) developed sophisticated classification systems. Sengyou (僧祐, 445-518), in his landmark *Chu sanzang ji ji* (出三藏記集, T2145, c. 515 CE), established categories including: new or old translations; anonymous and variant translations; spurious scriptures; **abridged scriptures** (抄經); extant and nonextant translations; Mahāyāna and Hīnayāna literature. These categories, as documented by Tokuno (1990) and Storch (2014), became standard in subsequent catalogues.

Significantly, Sengyou "spoke critically of abridged scripture redactions (chaojing 抄經), both of which were favoured by Emperor Wu (r. 502-549) of Liang" (Palumbo 2017, reviewing Storch). This tells us two things:

1. 抄經 were a recognized and distinct category of text, not mere translations or commentaries.
2. Even in the early 6th century, there was scholarly concern about the status of such texts. Sengyou's critical stance suggests that 抄經 compositions were viewed with suspicion precisely because they involved human editorial selection from sacred texts--they were not "the Buddha's own words" in the way that translations of Indian originals claimed to be.

Storch (2014) argues that Sengyou was "driven chiefly by a zeal for orthodoxy," which led him to be suspicious of unattributed texts and critical of excerpt compilations. This orthodoxy-driven suspicion of 抄經 makes it even less likely that a reviser operating within this cultural framework would *delete* doctrinally correct content from an existing digest text. The bibliographic tradition prized fidelity and completeness, not brevity.

Dao'an (道安, 312-385), whose earlier catalogue survives only in excerpts within Sengyou's work, established the influential "Five Deviations" (五失本, wǔ shīběn) and "Three Difficulties" (三不易, sān bùyì) framework for translation. The Five Deviations identify five ways translations can go wrong, including "changing the word order," "adding literary embellishment," and "eliminating repetitiveness." The Three Difficulties address the challenges of communicating the Dharma across cultures. Dao'an's emphasis throughout is on *fidelity* to source material. His concern was that translators might *add* embellishment or *cut* repetition for stylistic reasons--not that they might delete doctrinal content.

## Part II: Documented 抄經 Practices and Revision Patterns

### 2.1 Pattern 1: Extraction with Minimal Alteration

When excerpting from a source text, compilers typically:
- Select passages verbatim or near-verbatim
- Preserve doctrinal content intact
- Add connecting phrases or introductory frames
- Update terminology only when translation conventions have changed

**Example: Prajñāpāramitā Excerpts**

The Large Prajñāpāramitā (T223, T220) spawned numerous shorter texts. The typical pattern was *selection*, not *abridgment*. Compilers chose which passages to include; they did not edit the internal content of selected passages.

### 2.2 Pattern 2: Revision for Terminology Update

When creating a new version of an existing text, revisers typically:
- Update obsolete translation terms (e.g., 五陰 → 五蘊)
- Harmonize with current doctrinal standards
- Correct perceived errors
- **Add** clarifying material

Revisers do **not** typically:
- Delete coherent doctrinal elaborations
- Remove content that is doctrinally correct
- Abridge for mere length reduction

**Rationale:** Buddhist texts are sacred. The Chinese Buddhist canon was "an object of worship and devotion for whole Buddhist communities" and "represented the ultimate teaching of the Buddha and the Buddha's body." Different translations of the same sūtra were "safely preserved in the Chinese Tripitaka in their respective original versions without their being constantly revised according to later translations, as was the case with Tibetan scriptures" (Digital English Catalogue, Taishō). Deleting doctrinal content that is not doctrinally *wrong* would be disrespectful to the dharma and serve no purpose.

### 2.3 Pattern 3: Liturgical Adaptation

For chanting or devotional use, texts might be:
- Shortened by selecting key passages
- Repetitive sections condensed
- Non-essential narrative removed

But again, this involves *selection* (choosing what to include), not *editing* (deleting parts of selected passages).

### Case Study Details

#### Case 3: T223 to T220 (Prajñāpāramitā Retranslation)

Kumārajīva's T223 (404 CE) and Xuanzang's T220 (660s CE) are both translations of Large Prajñāpāramitā material. Comparing them:

| Feature | T223 (Kumārajīva) | T220 (Xuanzang) |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Terminology | 五陰, 觀世音 | 五蘊, 觀自在 |
| Content | Full doctrinal exposition | Full doctrinal exposition |
| Deletions | -- | None observed |
| Additions | -- | More complete in some sections |

Xuanzang did not delete doctrinal content from Kumārajīva's version; he produced a fresh translation with updated terminology. Jan Nattier has shown that apparent "abbreviations" in Kumārajīva's translations were not deliberate cuts but rather reflections of earlier Indic source manuscripts that had not yet undergone expansion: "When earlier Indic-language manuscript fragments are available, they often provide exact parallels of Kumārajīva's supposed 'abbreviations'."

#### Case 5-6: Vimalakīrtinirdeśa Three Translations

Three canonical Chinese versions survive: T474 (Zhi Qian, 3rd c.), T475 (Kumārajīva, 406), T476 (Xuanzang, 650). The differences between them reflect translation philosophy, not editorial deletion:

- **Zhi Qian** (T474): Elegant, literary style; possible Dharmarakṣa revision
- **Kumārajīva** (T475): Translated "for meaning"; idiomatic Chinese; most popular
- **Xuanzang** (T476): "cumbersome and overly literal," faithful to Sanskrit structure

Kumārajīva's is shorter than Xuanzang's in many places, but this reflects different source manuscripts and translation philosophies, not deliberate deletion of doctrine. As Jean-Noël Robert has shown, Kumārajīva "made extensive use of the earlier version," borrowing readings from Dharmarakṣa's version rather than deleting from it.

#### Case 7: Sukhāvatīvyūha Textual Growth (24 to 48 Vows)

The Larger Sukhāvatīvyūha Sūtra survives in seven versions with dramatically different vow counts:

| Version | Translator | Date | Vow Count |
|---------|-----------|------|-----------|
| Da Amituo jing | Zhi Qian | 3rd c. | 24 vows |
| Pingdengjue jing | Zhi Loujiachen | 3rd c. | 24 vows |
| Wuliangshou jing | Saṃghavarman | 252 | 48 vows |
| Sanskrit | -- | -- | 47 vows |
| Tibetan | -- | -- | 47 vows |

The direction of change is unmistakable: **expansion from 24 to 48 vows**. Studies have shown that the Pingdengjue jing "serves as a revision grounded in the Da Amituo jing while retaining the twenty-four vow structure." Later versions expanded to 48 vows. No version *reduces* the number of vows. The textual tradition grows; it does not contract by deleting doctrinally correct material.

#### Case 8: Golden Light Sūtra Expansion

The Suvarṇaprabhāsa Sūtra provides one of the clearest examples of textual expansion over time:

| Version | Translator | Date | Chapters |
|---------|-----------|------|----------|
| T663 | Dharmakṣema | early 5th c. | 13 |
| T664 | Baogui (composite) | 597 CE | Supplemented gaps from T663 |
| T665 | Yijing | 703 CE | 31 |

The original text "has undergone many modifications and its bulk has swelled with the passing of time." Additions include the Jātaka of the hungry tigress, an exposition of Śūnyatā, political ideas of kingship, and a medical chapter. No version removes content present in an earlier version; the direction is consistently additive.

#### Case 9: Lotus Sūtra and the Devadatta Chapter

Kumārajīva's Lotus Sūtra (T262, 406 CE) was **missing** the Devadatta chapter that had been present in Dharmarakṣa's earlier version. However, this was not a deliberate deletion of doctrine. The chapter was absent from Kumārajīva's Sanskrit source manuscript. Crucially, when the gap was recognized, the chapter was **later restored** to Kumārajīva's text from other sources. This episode demonstrates the cultural imperative: when content was discovered to be missing, the tradition *added it back* rather than accepting the shorter version as definitive.

#### Case 10: Ratnaguṇasaṃcayagāthā

The Ratnaguṇasaṃcayagāthā is a verse summary (302 verses) of the Aṣṭasāhasrikā Prajñāpāramitā, possibly older than the prose version. It condenses the teaching into verse form but preserves doctrinal content. The condensation operates at the level of literary form (prose to verse), not doctrinal deletion.

## Part IV: Heart Sūtra Commentaries as Evidence

### 4.1 7th-Century Commentary Evidence

7th-century commentaries (Kuiji T1710, Woncheuk T1711) document variant Heart Sūtra readings. When discussing variants, commentators:
- Note differences in wording
- Discuss which reading is preferable
- **Never** suggest that a variant arose from deleting correct doctrine

Woncheuk's commentary is particularly relevant: it notes variant readings including the 苦惱 variant documented in T250. This shows that even in the 7th century, scholars were aware of different Heart Sūtra textual traditions, but they explained the differences as alternative readings, not as deliberate deletions.

This suggests that in the 7th-century Buddhist understanding, deletion of correct doctrine was not a recognized revision pattern.

### 4.2 Dhāraṇī and Mantra Transmission Patterns

Dhāraṇī collections show similar patterns:
- Mantras are transmitted carefully
- Variants typically involve *additions* (e.g., adding oṃ, adding epithets)
- *Deletions* of mantra elements are rare and typically represent transmission error, not deliberate revision

The Heart Sūtra mantra itself shows this pattern: later versions *add* oṃ; no version *deletes* elements present in earlier versions. The epithet tradition shows the same pattern: T251 *adds* a fourth epithet (大神咒) to the three in T250/T223; no version reduces the number below three.

## Part V: The Direction of Change in Buddhist Textual History

### 5.1 Conze's Model of Prajñāpāramitā Development

Edward Conze's influential nine-stage model of Prajñāpāramitā literature describes a pattern of expansion followed by contraction:

1. Base urtext (Ratnaguṇasaṃcayagāthā, first two chapters)
2. Expansion to Aṣṭasāhasrikā (8,000 lines)
3. Further expansion to 18,000, 25,000, and 100,000 lines
4. Contraction into shorter sūtras (Diamond Sūtra, Heart Sūtra, down to the Prajñāpāramitā in One Letter)

The "contraction" phase, however, involves **new composition** from source material, not editorial deletion from existing texts. The Diamond Sūtra and Heart Sūtra are not abridgements of the 25,000-line version; they are new compositions that select and transform passages from the larger tradition. The Prajñāpāramitā in One Letter (the single syllable "A") is not a deletion of everything else; it is a creative distillation.

This distinction is crucial: the 抄經 tradition creates new compositions by selection and synthesis. It does not revise existing compositions by deleting their content.

### 5.2 Comprehensive Pattern Summary

| Direction of Change | Frequency in Documented Cases | Examples |
|--------------------|------------------------------|----------|
| Expansion (adding vows, chapters, epithets) | Very common | Sukhāvatīvyūha (24→48 vows), Golden Light (13→31 ch.), Heart Sūtra epithets (3→4) |
| Terminology update | Very common | 五陰→五蘊, 觀世音→觀自在, 明呪→咒 |
| Fresh retranslation from different source | Common | Vimalakīrti three versions, Pañcaviṃśati versions |
| Restoration of missing material | Documented | Lotus Sūtra Devadatta chapter restored |
| New digest composition from source | Common | Heart Sūtra(s), other 抄經 texts |
| **Deletion of doctrinally correct content from existing text** | **Not documented** | -- |

## Part VI: What Revisers Actually Do

Based on documented 抄經 and revision practice across ten case studies, when a reviser creates a new version of a Buddhist text, they typically:

| Action | Frequency | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Update terminology | Very common | 五陰 → 五蘊, 觀世音 → 觀自在 |
| Add clarifying phrases | Common | Additional epithets, 一切, etc. |
| Add frame narratives | Common | Long recension development |
| Expand doctrinal content | Common | 24→48 vows, 13→31 chapters |

| Correct perceived errors | Occasional | Grammatical improvements |
| Delete erroneous content | Rare | Only if doctrinally wrong |

## Part VII: Applying the Evidence to T250/T251

The T250 passages absent from T251:

1. **Skandha characteristics:**
   > 色空故無惱壞相，受空故無受相，想空故無知相，行空故無作相，識空故無覺相。

   This is doctrinally correct elaboration on how emptiness affects each skandha. It is not erroneous, heterodox, or redundant. A reviser would have no reason to delete it.

2. **Temporal negation:**
   > 是空法，非過去，非未來，非現在。

   This is standard Prajñāpāramitā doctrine. It adds precision without contradiction. A reviser would have no reason to delete it.

3. **苦惱 addition:**
   > 離一切顛倒夢想苦惱

   The word 苦惱 (afflictions/suffering) is doctrinally appropriate. Its presence in T250 and absence from T251 fits the parallel composition model better than the revision model.

### 7.1 The Revision Model's Burden

If T251 is a revision of T250 (or vice versa), the reviser would have had to:
- Delete the five-skandha characteristic passage (doctrinally correct)
- Delete the temporal negation (doctrinally correct)
- Remove 苦惱 from the bodhisattva-result passage (doctrinally correct)

None of these deletions can be explained by:
- Doctrinal error (the passages are standard Prajñāpāramitā)
- Terminological obsolescence (the terms are not outdated)
- Sectarian disagreement (no school objects to these teachings)
- Length constraints (the Heart Sūtra is already extremely short; a few lines make negligible difference)

In the documented 抄經 tradition, no parallel case of such deletion has been found.

### 7.2 The Parallel Composition Model's Explanation

Under the parallel composition model, the differences are natural:
- T250 and T251 each independently selected passages from T223
- T250 included the skandha-characteristic passage; T251 did not select it
- T250 included the temporal negation; T251 did not select it
- The composers made different choices about what to extract, reflecting different compositional aims or contexts

This is precisely what we would expect from two independent 抄經 compositions from the same source.

## Part VIII: Counter-Evidence and Devil's Advocate

### 8.1 Possible Counter-Arguments

**Objection 1: "Gunaprabha condensed the Vinaya."**

Gunaprabha (7th century) condensed the seventeen topics of the Mūlasarvāstivāda Vinaya into a shorter *Vinayasūtra*. This is a genuine case of scholarly condensation. However, it is a **śāstra** (treatise), not a **sūtra** (scripture). Treatises are human compositions; sūtras claim to be the Buddha's own words. The cultural taboo against deleting content applies more strongly to sūtras than to treatises. Moreover, Gunaprabha's condensation involved reorganization and reformulation of disciplinary rules, not deletion of doctrinal passages from a sacred text.

**Objection 2: "Theravādins may have omitted Mahāyāna-friendly passages."**

Some scholars have suggested that Theravādin communities may have omitted content that supported Mahāyāna positions. However, Peter Harvey has argued that "while the Theravādans may have added texts to their Tipiṭaka (such as the Abhidhamma texts), they generally did not tamper with the earlier material." Moreover, even if sectarian deletion occurred in the Theravāda context, this would involve deletion of *doctrinally objectionable* content (from the Theravāda perspective), not deletion of content that both parties accepted as correct. The T250 passages absent from T251 are not sectarianly controversial.

**Objection 3: "Kumārajīva's Lotus Sūtra is missing the Devadatta chapter."**

As documented above, this was not a deliberate deletion. Kumārajīva's Sanskrit source manuscript lacked the chapter. When the gap was recognized, the chapter was restored. This actually *reinforces* the argument: the tradition's response to missing content was to *add it back*, not to accept the shorter version.

**Objection 4: "Perhaps T251 was composed for liturgical brevity."**

The Heart Sūtra is already one of the shortest texts in the Buddhist canon (260 characters in T251). The difference between T250 and T251 is perhaps 30-40 characters. If liturgical brevity were the goal, the savings would be negligible. Moreover, liturgical abbreviation typically targets repetitive formulae and narrative framing, not doctrinal elaborations.

**Objection 5: "Xuanzang's translation philosophy favoured conciseness."**

This is anachronistic. Xuanzang's translations are characteristically *more* complete and literal than Kumārajīva's, not less. Xuanzang's style "prioritized faithfulness to the original text, ensuring the integrity of the content." If Xuanzang (or someone in his circle) composed T251, their philosophy would have favoured *including* more content, not deleting it.

### 8.2 Strongest Counter-Argument: Accidental Omission vs. Deliberate Deletion

The strongest counter-argument is not deliberate deletion but **accidental omission**: a copyist's eye might have skipped the skandha-characteristic passage through homoioteleuton (similar endings) or simple scribal error. However, this explanation faces its own difficulties:

1. The omission of multiple distinct passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation, 苦惱) from a single text is unlikely to be a single scribal accident.
2. The omissions are consistently of *additional doctrinal content*, not random text loss.
3. The systematic nature of T251's terminology differences (觀自在 vs. 觀世音, 五蘊 vs. 五陰, 舍利子 vs. 舍利弗) cannot be explained by scribal error and points to independent composition.

### 8.3 Honest Assessment

The argument from absence of counter-examples is inherently limited. We cannot prove that deletion of doctrinal content *never* happened; we can only show that it is *not documented* in the cases we have examined. The argument is probabilistic, not deductive.

Furthermore, the 抄經 tradition is not fully catalogued. Hundreds of digest texts were composed in China, and a systematic survey of all surviving examples has not been conducted. It remains possible that counter-examples exist in texts we have not examined.

### 9.2 Relevant Secondary Literature

Funayama Tōru's work on the boundary "between Chinese translation and Chinese composition" is particularly relevant. He has shown that once Chinese monks recognized the characteristic elements of Buddhist translation style, they adopted this style in their own writings. This describes precisely the compositional practice behind 抄經: a Chinese author writes in Buddhist translation style, selecting and arranging passages from existing translations. Funayama's framework supports the view that T250 and T251 are both products of this compositional practice, each author making independent choices about what to extract and how to arrange it.

## Part X: The Negative Case Argument (Revised)

**Premise 1:** In ten documented cases of Buddhist text revision, retranslation, and digest composition spanning the 3rd through 8th centuries CE, the consistent direction of change is expansion, addition, terminology update, and restoration. Deletion of doctrinally correct content is not documented in any case.

**Premise 2:** T250 contains doctrinally correct passages (skandha characteristics, temporal negation, 苦惱) absent from T251.

**Premise 3:** These passages are not erroneous, heterodox, redundant, or sectarianly objectionable by any known standard.

**Premise 4:** The bibliographic tradition (Sengyou, Dao'an) emphasised textual fidelity, completeness, and orthodoxy. The sacred status of Buddhist scriptures ("the Buddha's body") created a cultural imperative against deletion.

**Conclusion:** T251 is unlikely to be a revision of T250 (or vice versa). The absence of these passages is better explained by parallel composition: T250 and T251 were composed independently from T223, each compiler making different choices about which passages to extract.

## Part XI: Limitations and Desiderata

This analysis would be strengthened by:

1. **Systematic survey of 抄經 texts**: A comprehensive catalogue of all surviving Chinese Buddhist digest texts, with source identification and comparison of revision patterns. This is a major desideratum for the field.

2. **Japanese 写経 (shakyō) traditions**: Japanese sutra copying (写経, shakyō) traditions may preserve intermediate versions. The Japanese *rufubon* (流布本) version of the Heart Sūtra shows minor additions (一切 added) but no deletions, consistent with the pattern documented here.

3. **Dunhuang manuscript evidence**: Variant Heart Sūtra manuscripts from Dunhuang (S.700, S.2464, P.2168, P.2323, etc.) may reveal revision patterns. Preliminary evidence shows T251-type texts dominating, with T250-type texts surviving as a minority.

4. **INBUDS/CiNii search**: Japanese scholarship on 抄經 revision practices in journals such as *Bukkyōgaku kenkyū* and *Indogaku Bukkyōgaku kenkyū* has not been systematically surveyed for this research.

5. **Full Taishō catalogue of 抄經 texts**: Fei Changfang's *Lidai sanbao ji* (T2034, 598 CE) and Zhisheng's *Kaiyuan shijiao lu* (T2154, 730 CE) contain classifications that could yield additional 抄經 examples.

6. **The *Zhongjing bielu* (眾經別錄)**: This lost 5th-century catalogue, partially recoverable from Dunhuang fragments (P.3737, S.2872), may contain early classification of 抄經 texts.

## Conclusion

Based on documented practice across ten case studies spanning five centuries of Chinese Buddhist textual history, the consistent pattern is clear:

- **Expansion** is the dominant direction of change (24→48 vows, 13→31 chapters, 3→4 epithets)
- **Terminology update** is very common (五陰→五蘊, 觀世音→觀自在)
- **Restoration** of missing material is documented (Devadatta chapter)
- **Deletion of doctrinally correct content** is not documented in any case examined

The T250 passages absent from T251 are doctrinally correct elaborations. Their absence is difficult to explain under the revision model but natural under the parallel composition model: two independent composers, each extracting from T223, made different choices about which passages to include.

This does not *prove* parallel composition, but it shifts the burden: if T251 revised T250 (or vice versa), what motivated the deletion of correct doctrine? The documented 抄經 tradition provides no precedent for such an action.
